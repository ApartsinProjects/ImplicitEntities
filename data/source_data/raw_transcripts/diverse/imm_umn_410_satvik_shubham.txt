Description:
Satvick tells the story of his multiple migrations and how they have impacted him.

Transcript:

This story is part of the Immigrant Stories project — a digital storytelling initiative by the Immigration History Research Center at the University of Minnesota. The goal is to preserve and share personal narratives about migration, identity and belonging. My story isn’t about just one move or a single cultural shift. It’s about growing up in motion — across eight different countries — and trying to make sense of where I fit in a world that never stayed still. [MAIN STORY] My name is Satvik, and though I was born in India, I moved to Bhutan just six months later. That move — my first — set the tone for a life that’s never stood still. Since then, I’ve lived in Bhutan, England, Indonesia, Germany, India again, Finland, the Czech Republic, and now, the United States. Each place was like a new chapter in a book I didn’t know I was writing. I didn’t always have a choice — most moves were shaped by my parents’ work or education. But with each one, I learned to adapt quickly: new customs, new food, new ways of greeting, new rules in classrooms, and new meanings behind simple things. I had to grow up fast. I had to listen before I spoke. I had to figure out who I was in a world that kept changing. India is where I was born — my beginning. It’s where my family comes from, and where the language and traditions that ground me first entered my life. But I left it before I could even walk. Bhutan, my first home, is where I learned stillness — a country defined by peace and simplicity. I remember misty mountains, quiet roads, and the comfort of knowing few people, but knowing them well. In England, everything was different — structured, formal, familiar but foreign. It was the first place I attended school, where I had to wear a uniform, queue politely, and adjust to gray skies and different spellings of familiar words. Jakarta, Indonesia, was bursting with energy. The streets were alive — with scooters, food carts, people. It was loud, friendly, full of movement. I played barefoot soccer in alleys and learned how warm and open a city could be. Frankfurt, Germany, was order and precision. I moved from the chaos of Jakarta to the calm of trams and punctuality. People gave you space. Conversations were deliberate. It was one of the hardest adjustments I made — slowing down, fitting in. Moving from Jakarta to Frankfurt was one of the biggest shifts I’ve experienced. In Jakarta, life spilled out onto the streets. In Frankfurt, everything was contained — structured, neat, on time. I had to learn to slow down, to read the silences, to fit in without losing myself. When we moved back to India, I expected to feel at home again. But I didn’t — not fully. After so much time abroad, I had changed and so had the way I saw everything around me. I appreciated things more, but I also felt distant from people who hadn’t moved. Helsinki, Finland, gave me quiet — and the space to think. It taught me that silence doesn’t mean disinterest, that deep relationships can grow slowly and steadily, like a winter thaw. In the Czech Republic, I felt a growing independence. I wandered Prague’s streets alone, discovering a city steeped in stories and history. It was a place that invited curiosity — and let me be myself without explanation. And now I’m in the United States, pursuing my education. It’s a place that prizes individuality but also asks you — constantly — to define yourself. Where are you from? What’s your story? Who are you? Living in so many places has given me a rare kind of education. I’ve learned how deeply culture shapes people — how even small things like tone or gestures can mean something completely different across borders. I’ve become good at adjusting, at observing, at reading the room. But even with all this knowledge, there’s a quiet weight I carry — the sense that I don’t fully belong anywhere. In India, I’m not quite local. In the U.S., I’m still the international student. And in Europe, I was often just passing through. Wherever I go, I’m familiar — but not fully rooted. I’ve collected so many pieces of identity, but they don’t always fit into one picture. Still, I wouldn’t trade this journey for anything. It’s made me more open-minded, more empathetic, more aware. I can walk into almost any room and find something to connect with. But there are still days when someone asks me, “Where are you from?” and I hesitate — not because I don’t know, but because there’s no simple answer. My story isn’t just about moving between countries. It’s about what I’ve carried with me — fragments of culture, language, friendships, loss, and learning. It’s about discovering that identity isn’t always rooted in place — sometimes, it’s shaped through motion. I may not belong fully to one culture. But I belong to the journey — and maybe, that’s enough.
