Description:
Anna Xiong was born in Thailand in 1994. She and her family came to the United States in 2004.  She is a student at Century College.

Transcript:

Anna Xiong Transcription “Hmong basket in Laos” Hi, my name is Anna Xiong. I am going to talk about my father’s basket. My parents lived in Laos. My parents didn’t go to the United States because they might thought something would happen to us. [A] few Hmong people came to the United States and several of them came to live in Thailand. I was born in Thailand. My parents—we came to the United States in 2004. There were many types of bamboo baskets to carry. My father loved to make bamboo baskets. He always makes them for my mom and I. We carried those basket behind our backs to go to a farm. Often, its contents [were] grain and pieces of wood on her back. The basket comes with everything. Without it, we can’t hold everything in his hands. My father and his siblings loved to make baskets for their wives. They gather around to take turns to help each other. They can finish quickly. My father had been taught to make the baskets since he was a kid. My mom always put chicken wrapped with banana leaves and some chile pepper put in the basket. My father loves spicy food so much. The husbands made baskets to carry things. The wives sewed paj ntaub to earn money for their family. When my we were in Thailand, my mom taught me how to sew paj ntaub. I can make more money to help my parents. Hmong people only paid twenty dollars for it. The basket is still important today. We still use the basket to show the audience what it does. We want the new generation to learn about it. In Hmong New Year, some girls dance with the baskets and dance with them.
