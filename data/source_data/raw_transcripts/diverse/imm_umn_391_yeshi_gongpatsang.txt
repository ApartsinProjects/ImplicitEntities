Description:
Yeshi Gongpatsang was forced to flee her home country of Tibet in 1969 when the Chinese Communist Party invaded. When she came to the U.S., she still risked her life by doing a hunger strike to raise awareness about injustices in Tibet.

Transcript:

My mom had a very difficult life, and the way she persevered through everything is the reason I am everything today. She was born in Kham, Tibet in 1969 and was forced to flee in 1985 as the Chinese Communist Party invaded Tibet and began its occupation of the land and took the freedom of the Tibetan people. She fled by foot to India, and there she worked for many years until she won the lottery of the immigration act of 1990, allowing 1,000 Tibetan refugees into America. My mom left by herself because her family was too scared of the punishments that the Chinese government would give them if they were caught. She left behind her mother, 5 siblings, her entire childhood, and everyone and everything she had ever known. She went with smugglers and other random people that were trying to escape as well, and she eventually made new friends as she went throughout her journey. She was so scared when she first left because this was her first time being away from any family members and she had never left home before. The journey was also very scary as she had to climb mountains and avoid patrols and not get caught. Once she arrived to India, she had to learn to grow up and become an independent woman. She had never worked for money and was not familiar with a job or spending habits, and had to learn quickly as she was all by herself. When my mom moved to the U.S, she had help from her sponsor that showed her the way and was there for her as she transitioned into her new life. It was still very hard learning an entirely new language and how different the lifestyle was in America compared to India and Tibet. She made new friends and eventually made a life in America. Just because my mom had finally made it to America, it did not mean that she forgot her past. She came to America not just for a better life for herself, but for a better life for all Tibetan people. She still talks to her family on the phone almost every single day and has been an active supporter of the Free Tibet movement. She even risked her life back in 1990 when she did a hunger strike for 11 days, trying to raise awareness to the U.N. about the injustice in Tibet. My mom has beat the odds every time, has been through so much, and yet she continues to do her best to be a good parent and to smile every day. With all of her life experiences, this has inspired me to be a positive and caring person while having that same hard‐work ethic. I know that I want to be able to tell her to relax and give her time to enjoy the things she wanted to in her life.
