Transcript:

Born and raised in Egypt with four siblings, three brothers and one sister, Georgette Tadros was an extremely academic person. She had a customary French education and was top of her class. (Interview:) 0:47 - 1:06 Georgette was on the beach one day when she caught the eye of a man named Adly Iskandar, who thought she was extremely beautiful and asked around to find out who she was. As was tradition in Egypt, he approached Georgette's father to ask for her hand in marriage, despite having never even spoken to her before. Her father agreed, and they entered the "engagement period," where the engaged couple gets to know each other. Despite being extremely against the marriage at first, Georgette eventually fell for him, and changed her name to Georgette Iskandar after they were married. Close to her marriage, Georgette received a scholarship to get her phD in Paris at an elite university, and she studied a French writer for her thesis. She lived in Paris for 10 years, and during that time, her husband became a doctor. Georgette and Adly went back and forth between Paris and London, where Adly worked, before eventually deciding to move to the US in 1969. There were several reasons why they decided to move, one being the tense religious climate back home in Egypt. Egypt was about half Islam and half Christian, and due to the growing conflicts, they felt they'd be better off in the US. They were also led there by Adly's career, after he received a job offer to become a doctor in Boston. However, he had to redo his residency, as his Egyptian and English residencies weren't valid in the US. Georgette and Adly were able to get to the US very easily, since both were highly educated and contributed to the work force. They went through the proper channels to get green cards, and it didn't end up being a complicated process to gain citizenship. However, they were luckier than others. Thousands of Egyptians immigrated to the US between 1967 and 1977 after Egypt's defeat in the 6-Day War, during which it fought with Israel over Israeli shipping rights, and wrecked its own economy in the process. The US made reforms to prejudical immigration laws in 1965, allowing more Egyptians to immigrate, but later decreased the number in 1980. Newer immigrants were sometimes barred from communities made up of older immigrants, and language barriers didn't help their advancement. However, Middle Eastern immigrants were extremely well educated, even causing a "brain drain" in the Middle East when a huge number of educated people left for the States. Georgette worked as a trilingual translator for immigration courts, speaking Arabic, French and English, and as a docent at the Museum of Fine Arts in Boston. Later, she was a very successful realtor. Georgette and Adly were among the first in their families to move to the US, but both families slowly came over, and Georgette's brother started a date ranch in Palm Springs that kept the family together. Georgette was also good at finding Egyptian and French communities in Boston, which she became a part of. (Interview:) 9:02 - 9:50 Georgette passed away in 2021 with two children and six grandchildren, and she was surrounded by her family until the very end. She is buried in Boston next to her husband, and she will live on for generations to come.
