Description:
Derwin Halim was born in Jakarta, Indonesia in 1980. After graduating from high school, he moved to Minnesota in 1998 to pursue higher education, joining his older sister who was already living and studying in Minnesota. He studied at Normandale Community College and the University of Minnesota, where graduated in 2002 with a degree in electrical engineering. He continues to live in Minnesota and work as an engineer.

Transcript:

Derwin Halim Transcription My name is Derwin Halim. I was born in Jakarta, Indonesia in 1980. I moved here in 1998 after high school for higher education. So, I went to a community college, Normandale Community College, which was also the same community college that my sister went to when she moved here. Just because it’s cheaper for tuition costs and everything. So the idea is spend two years at community college and take all your basic classes and requirements or liberal arts or everything like that and then spend the rest of the two years at the U. So that’s what I did. So I spent two years at Normandale and then I spent the next two years at the U for a four-year degree. Back home, in terms of the national curriculum, English is one of the requirements. So we do learn English as part of regular schoolwork. And then, outside of schoolwork, I think I did go to some English group lessons. So that’s how I prepared myself from the language perspective. I’d never read a full textbook in English, right, in terms of like math, biology, or physics even. I never read such a thing before or I never encountered such a thing before. So, my first two years at Normandale really kinda like eased me up into it. During my two years at the U, people said that hey, Win, it’s good to get internship if you are thinking of working here. And, at the time, it was like, oh yeah, sure, I don’t… getting experience and- hopefully in a respectful US company- wouldn’t hurt your chances if you want to go back home. So I was looking for an internships. I did get it and it was a two-term internships type of thing, and after the end of the internship, the company decided to hire me. Or put me an offer. So I was like, ok, great! So I accepted that. And that’s it. So they let me go in 2006. And I got another job at the company that I’m still working [for] right now. Back when I was let go, I either have to go back home, go back to school, or find another job. So those are kinda my three options. And I did explore all three. And it just so happened that I got an offer from the company where I work right now. And even when I worked for this new company, I gotta like… pay the dues or whatever. I can’t just walk in and say: hey! Sponsor me! Right, I gotta like pay my dues. So I waited for maybe a year, a year and a half. So that’s already like three or four years gone by right? And then, applying [for] the green card and the waiting process is also- it’s a long process, at least for me. Six, seven years. When you apply for a green card, there are, you wanna call it like different hierarchy, different slots, where you can be in. EB-3 is just like bachelor’s degree, college degree, or something. It’s kinda like where a bunch of people got in on the EB-3 slot. That’s where I was. So there’s a lot of applicants and long period, long wait time. Growing up, I remember my dad was like, really putting a strong emphasis on education because we’re not like a well-connected family that had like business connections or work connections all over the place. Like, oh yeah, you wanna work? Ok, you go see my friend and then work for him or her and then whatever, you know. So, I think, my dad’s point of view is if you wanna be successful, or if you want to be able to stand up on your own two feet, you gotta have something, right? And education is the way to go.
