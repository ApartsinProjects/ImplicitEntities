Description:
Food has always been a part of Emilee Xayanourom's life. She grew up making and eating authentic Lao dishes, and now owns a food truck with her family so that she can share her love of food with others in Minnesota.

Transcript:

Growing up, food was the center of my family, as many Asian cultures value food in ways other cultures don't. I grew up constantly in the kitchen with my dad or aunt and always found myself wanting to help in any way I could. Starting, I got to use the mortar to mash the tomatoes for papaya salad, moving into making sticky rice, and now I am fully immersed in the kitchen trying to learn how to make authentic Lao dishes by my dad's side. Food has always been more than just nutritional value to my family. Food is a vital component of cultural identity. Some of the most important lessons that my dad has taught me have been when we are cooking or eating at the dinner table, from always plating and serving others before yourself, to the appreciation you should have for your food and what a privilege it is to eat good food. My dad is a refugee from Laos and came to the United States in 1990. He has told me many stories about his journey here and his early experiences in the United States. A lot of those stories are centered around food. When he first settled into Minnesota, he took a trip to Texas to spend a whole month learning authentic Lao and Thai food and now he has a book full of recipes to teach me. Now my family owns a food truck where we can share our favorite foods to the communities that support us. Whether it is in the food truck at a town festival, or in my home kitchen my favorite part is that no matter what he is cooking, he wants to pass it down to my brother and me. As traditional food beliefs are transferred from generation to generation, he sees the value in passing down traditions, even if it is as little as knowing how to make papaya salad or sticky rice. Food brings us together. In a country which has known real hunger, Lao people want to be able to offer you food. Even the greeting ‘Sabaidee’ is often replaced by ‘Ginn khao’ or ‘Have you eaten already?’. Most people will try to make sure they have something to offer visitors. Food is truly a huge part of Lao culture, and I am glad to have grown up in a family who values and appreciates food for what it is.
