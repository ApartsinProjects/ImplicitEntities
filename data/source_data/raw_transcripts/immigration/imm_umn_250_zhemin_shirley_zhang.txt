12659_Justine_Hu__export_script.txt[1/25/21, 11:01:14 AM] “Xin Nian Kuai Le.” My mother, Zhang Zhemin, or “Shirley,” as everyone here knows her, loves to teach her coworkers, her friends, and her neighbors how to say “Happy New Year” in Mandarin. For as long as I can remember, she has been sharing her language and her culture with others. My mother left Shanghai, China in January 1997 to join her husband Hu Xiaobing, or Shelby, who had moved to Rochester, New York, one year prior for work. He had arrived with little money, no car, no house, and no English knowledge, but she had him. She began taking English as a Second Language (or ESL) classes before raising twin daughters, my sister Emily and I, in Rochester. 1-2 percent of the population in Rochester is Chinese. Although we have family friends who live in Chinatowns as well as Chinese ethnoburbs, my family settled in a suburban white neighborhood in Upstate New York. With few Chinese families nearby, none in our neighborhood, and none at my school, my mother actively sought to preserve her, and our, Chinese identity. She watches Chinese television and news, and only cooks Chinese cuisine. We celebrate traditional holidays with just the four of us, attending performances run by international students at local universities and eating duck on Thanksgiving. We were fortunate enough to find a community through a Chinese language school which my sister and I attended for 12 years. My mother immediately engaged in the life of the community, serving as treasurer for years before becoming principal of the over 200- student nonprofit. She met some of her best friends through it, later joining a “ladies’ club” that met to eat and gossip, and even hosted a large gathering for Shanghainese families to meet. We joke that she knows every Chinese person in Rochester. It’s probably true. Beyond being possibly the most active member of Rochester’s Chinese community, my mother adores sharing our culture with the broader community. Every year, she attends events at community centers, art galleries, and elementary schools to teach Chinese language, art, and customs. Watching her excitedly engage with her non-Chinese neighbors, it is clear that being Chinese in white America isn’t all bad. Just last year I explained to her why my sister and I asked her to make PB & J sandwiches for our entire lives. “Other kids made fun of a Vietnamese boy because he brought noodles. They said, “ew! Worms! We didn’t want to be made fun of like that.” She was visibly hurt that we had been ashamed for 17 years. But she told us, “Share your food, let them try it, they will soon know that Chinese food is the best in the world. They, too, will want to be Chinese.” Unlike Chinese living in ethnic enclaves or ethnoburbs, we had to actively find and construct our place in white suburbia. Today, my mother speaks fluent English, works at a public high school, and actively displays her identity in both the Chinese community and the broader local community. Even in adversity, she strives for harmony. Sometimes I wish I grew up in New York’s Chinatown or LA’s ethnoburbs so I could feel less different. But then I remember I wouldn’t have the unique family traditions my mother created, the deep connections she helped me forge, and the intense pride in being Chinese-American that she inspired with every “Xin Nian Kuai Le” she taught, and continues to teach.
