Description:
Motunrayo Olanrewaju was born in Ibadan, Nigeria in 1998. When she was ten, her parents won the Diversity Visa Lottery, and she immigrated to the United States with her parents and siblings. She grew up in Minnesota and studied at the University of Minnesota.

Transcript:

file:///F/Immigrant%20Stories%20Transfer/Olanrewaju,%20Tunrayo/Tunrayo%20Olanrewaju%20Transcription.txt[7/13/2017 1:04:08 PM] Hi, my name is Tunrayo Olanrewaju and I'm here to tell you about the ugly truth. I was born in Ibadan, Nigeria, and although Nigeria is considered a third-world country, I still had a good childhood. My dad was a lawyer or professor while my mom was a secretary at an electronic firm. When I was ten, my family decided to apply for the visa lottery. Although my mom did not want to come to America, my dad did, so he convinced her that they should apply. After a short while, we heard the news that we won the lottery. So then we packed up all our belongings and said good-bye to our family and October 30 boarded the plane to come to Minnesota. After the initial shock of the weather, we discovered that America wasn't perfect. In Nigeria, we'd heard stories about how America was an amazing place and how the people who came to America instantly became rich. I'd also watched TV shows like Barney which led me to think that America was somewhere where you could be anything and anyone. After a few months, my parents had to get jobs and also start school. My parents had to work jobs that they never would have taken in Nigeria. My parents had to work in nursing homes and they had to work weekends and holidays. This made our siblings and I very sad because we wanted to spend holidays with our parents. But they couldn't because they were working so hard. Because of the additional burden of taking care of four kids, my mom was not able to finish her degree, while my dad was able to get his associate's degree. The first few years for us in America were very hard because we were not prepared. We expected America to be easy and we discovered it wasn't. Many immigrants also come here and are so surprised by the hardships they face because they assume that in such a developed country like America being rich comes easily and there's no way to struggle. Although I do feel that the American Dream failed my parents, I hope that it won't fail me. I feel like the American Dream failed my parents because it's impossible to get up to the top. It seems like all there is to do is struggle at the bottom. My dad still longs to go back to Nigeria, while my mom has said that she would never give the advice to anyone to come to America. After I finished my college degree, I plan to go to law school and become a lawyer. I hope that my parents can see how coming to America has helped me achieve my dreams, and I hope that the American Dream does not fail me, like it did them. Thank you.
