Description:
Leelawadee tells the story of her mother, Wasana, who grew up in Thailand and immigrated to the U.S.

Transcript:

This is the story of my mother, Wasana Grimsby, or by her maiden name, Wasana Kraipinit. I will be telling details about the journey of her life to becoming a first generation immigrant in the United States. My mother grew up in the northeast of Thailand in this small town called Bahn Phai. There, she lived with her large family of 4 siblings. Her father passed away when she was 9, so at a young age she adopted the responsibility of taking care of her younger siblings. She has fond memories of Bahn Phai: telling me that although it was a small town, everybody was kind. People in Bahn Phai were close, sharing food, tools, and toys with each other. She lived in a two story wooden house, large enough to fit all her siblings. After high school, my mother left her hometown to attend university in Bangkok. There, she lived with her older sister in a small condo right in the heart of the city. She worked three jobs to put herself through school: selling lottery tickets, working at a call center, and picking up shifts around town. After 7 years, she graduated from Ramkhamhaeng University with a bachelor's degree in English Literature. After graduating, my mom stayed in Bangkok with her sister and continued working. She would return to her hometown every now and then to visit her mom and younger siblings. In 1998, she met my dad. He was in Thailand on a business trip. They were introduced to each other through mutual friends and quickly fell in love. My mother was worried about the big cultural shift that would come with moving Thailand to the United States. Then in 1999, my mother made the decision to marry my father and move to Ann Arbor, Michigan. In 2003, she gave birth to me and my brother. We were twins. In the US, my mom had a few jobs as a waitress but eventually became a stay at home mom. She always took care of my brother and I. My mom tells me that everything was a learning experience for her in the US. The culture, the social life, the food, and even the weather was different than she expected. Regardless of all the new changes, my mother tells me that she feels like the US is her new home. I admire my mom for her resilience. Despite the large changes she experienced in her life, she forged a home anywhere she went. Here in the US, she found a community through the Thai temple and new friends who share the immigrant experience. Most importantly, she thinks of the US as her home because our family is here. To my mom, home is where the family is. Her story has taught me a valuable lesson that wherever I go, as long as I am surrounded by the people I love, I will always have a place to call home.
