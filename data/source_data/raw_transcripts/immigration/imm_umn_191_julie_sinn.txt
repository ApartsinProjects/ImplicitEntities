Description:
Julie Sinn was born in Pusan, South Korea, in 1994.  For two month Julie was raised by a foster mother who named her Kyung Hwa Lee, Peaceful Good Event. In October 1994, Juliewas adpoted into a family in Saint Paul, Minnesota.

Transcript:

This is a story about two families. The family that gave me life and the family that taught me how to live it. My journey began on May 23, 1994 when I was born in a hospital for unwed mothers in Pusan, South Korea. I was born to [inaudible] and [inaudible], both in their early twenties and working at a department store. While I know their height and weight, and their number of siblings, my papers don’t say anything about the questions I have the most: what were their favorite breakfast foods, their dreams for the future, the sound of their laugh? For two months, I stayed with my foster mother: Mrs. Lee, and my caseworker named me after her: Kyung Hwa Lee: Peaceful Good Event. I have always found my name ironic: was my birth peaceful? truly a good event? My second journey began on October 29, 1994 when I flew from Busan, South Korea to Saint Paul, Minnesota. I was five months old, with a plastic wristband on my tiny baby wrist with my Korean name and my family’s new name written on the same lines. My mom remembers how quiet I was when I arrived---I didn’t cry. As family member after family member stood in line to take a picture with me, my face remained stoic, observant, reflective: wondering. To this day, I am still wondering. What would my life be like if I had grown up half a world apart? Would I yearn for my adoptive mother the same way I yearn for my biological mother like a body mourns the loss of a phantom limb, only feeling its presence in its absence? Does my foster mother remember me? The baby, peaceful good event who she rocked to sleep for 3 months and then never saw again? Whose good event was I? And did I bring anyone some peace? If I could could speak to my birth mother in this life, or write my birth mother a letter, I would have too many stories to tell, too many questions to ask, and too many stories in my heart. And while my heart is sometimes filled with both wondering and longing when I think about the life I never had, I find my heart is already brimming with gratitude for the life that I do. The only certain journey in my life is the one that I am on. The only certain people in my life? The ones who loved me all along the way. And I like to imagine it was my birth mother’s love for me and my family that helped her let me go. And as she wonders about what could have been, I wish I could write her a letter and tell her about what is: I would tell her about my sister's smile and the way that everyone thought we were twins even though we share no biological relation: that family is a feeling not a skin color or blood type. I would tell her about my dad’s laugh, funny faces, stable presence, and warm hugs---that I slept on his chest on the living room recliner on my first night in America because my dad thought I would be uncomfortable and lonely in my crib. And I would tell her about my adopted mother. My mother. And the way she sang me lullabies every night before bed, calming me as I drifted into safe dreams. And the way she carried me in her arms and never gave up on me and taught me patience, and genuine kindness. The way she didn’t give me life, but she taught me how to live it. If I could write my birth mother a letter, I would have too many stories to tell her, but they would all start with my family and end with “thank you, for letting your peaceful good event find peace and happiness right where she belonged.” ---that home is a feeling you find in your heart and when you’ve found it you just know.
