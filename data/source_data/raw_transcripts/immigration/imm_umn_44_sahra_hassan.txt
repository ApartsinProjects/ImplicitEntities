Description:
Sahra Hassan was born and grew up in Mogadishu, Somalia. In Somalia, she completed secondary school, a year of national service, and started her own business in 1985.  After the civil war, she left Somalia for Uganda. She came to the United States as a refugee in 2005 and lives in Minnesota.

Transcript:

Sahra H. Hassan Transcription Hello. My name is Sahra Hassan and this is my Immigrant Story. I was born and grew up in Mogadishu, Somalia. After my primary school, I attended and graduated from Wadajir secondary school in Mogadishu, Somalia in 1984. This is my high school diploma. When I finished my secondary school, I was sent to one year of national service, which was a mandate to every graduate. I was issued a national service identification card, and then I was assigned into an adult literacy school that was not far from my home where I was teaching Arabic language. This service gives me more privilege like any other student who completed the service. In 1985, instead of going to further my education, I decided to start my own business with the help of the Somali government. The national service gave me the opportunity to borrow from the bank and open up my own business which was grocery store, called in Somali “bagaash.” I was selling clothes, shoes, perfumes, and electronics. My business grew until the central government was over thrown by the rebels in 1991. Due to civil war, I left my country and sought asylum in Uganda. My national identification card was the most valuable things that migrated with me into Uganda. Why it is important for me? Because it is my national ID that reminds me of the lost central Somali government that used to hold together the entire country. I decided to preserve it as my own treasure and pass on to my children so that they can pass over their children too. When I was in Uganda, whenever I was asked to prove my identity, I used to produce it, and it helps me a lot because there were so many refugees from different countries. Why I left Uganda? The U.S.A. government gave me the opportunity to resettle in U.S. under the refugee program. However, when I moved to United States in 2005, I came Minnesota and I lived here since then. My national ID was one of the most valuable belongings that I carried to United States. It reminds me [of] back home and our lost glory that is Somali government. These memories lets me keep my ID forever and pass it over to my children. Therefore, this valuable object I presented to you is my beloved ID and thank you for all listening.
