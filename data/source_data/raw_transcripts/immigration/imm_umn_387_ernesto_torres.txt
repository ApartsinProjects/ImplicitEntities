Description:
Ernesto's granddaughter Isabel talks about her experienes being from an immigrant family. Then, Ernesto is interviewed about his life in the Philippines and his perspective on his life as an immigrant in the U.S.

Transcript:

Izzy: My name is Isabel or Isabel Laderman. I am the granddaughter of a Filipino immigrant and was born in Minneapolis, Minnesota in 2003. I didn’t realize that I wasn’t fully white, or what race was really until I was around 8 and someone asked me what my Grandpa was. I didn’t know how to answer except “my grandpa? He’s human.” Since then, I’ve learned more about my family’s history and experiences, especially about how that history impacts my life is currently via systemic racism and xenophobia. Generational trauma is a very real experience. At the same time, it’s because of my family’s resilience in the face of adversity that I have the privileges I do. My grandpa is a passionate and determined person and has done so much for me and my family. While I fully embrace myself as a mixed person and being from an immigrant family, I’m the epilogue to his story, so I asked my grandfather to tell his story via an interview. Izzy: Alright, hi Grandpa. So first, if you want to tell me your full name? Ernie: Ernesto Panci Panci Torres Ernie: I was born in the Philippines, in Bacolod City, Negros Island August 26, 1943. I was 19 years old, in February 1962. I arrive in San Fransisco. I was going to school in Bacolod run by the Christian brothers, and they had a lot of influence on me. They speak English in school and the only way to improve myself at that time was just to get out of, you know, where I was. And I had a lot of encouragement from them, the school. Even at that time, the schools were using American books, run by the Christian brothers, the same books they had in California. I was lucky in that way. But it was a very expensive school. The only way I could stay in that school was to work for my tuition. So I did that. So, I was working since I was nine. I never had a summer vacation. When the school ends, the next day I’m working. I work till the school opens again. That was ‐ you know, I didn’t know any better. Yes, it was a hard life, but that was the sacrifice I had to do to be in this great school. So I think its paid off. You know, immigrants are always looking for a better life. Immigrants we work hard to achieve that. You know, they sacrifice. The ultimate goals of immigrants I think, I’m one of them, that the ultimate goal is all about the kids. To have the kids have a better life. Immigrants sacrifice themselves for the kids to have a better education, to have a better life, that’s all it was; I mean for us. So in some ways, like for me, I accomplished that. With all the kids went to college, had a profession. Yeah. Yeah, that’s the accomplishment. Yeah, it’s done, you know. It’s done. So anything above that is extra.
