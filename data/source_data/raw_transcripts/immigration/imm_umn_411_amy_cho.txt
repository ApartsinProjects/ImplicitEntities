Description:
Amy tells the story of her grandfather who immigrated from South Korea

Transcript:

Hi, my name is Amy and this is my family’s immigrant story. You see me right now playing around with my friends, being silly, speaking English, growing up in America at a boarding school, and living my life as usual. But this life didn’t start with me. It started with my grandfather. With just a few dollars in his hand, surrounded by unfamiliar faces, my grandpa arrived in Los Angeles International Airport in 1981. He left South Korea with barely anything. He had just a small suitcase and a promise to his family: I’ll send money home. I’ll build a better life. He left his three kids and wife in South Korea to send them money from America. He never intended to stay long. America was nothing like home. Endless land, huge trees, fresh cold air that smelled like hope. My grandfather often helped out with our small family farm back in Korea, and the big farmland in America drew him in and assured him that his choice of coming to America was right. He started his life as a simple auto mechanic as he was always good with repairing anything. He came to America on a work visa. He lived in LA for around 1-2 years. But life was lonely. No family in LA. He has never been alone for so long. His aunt lived in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, which was very far from where he was, he wanted to get closer to a relative. She was quite wealthy as she ran a sock company. In America a common way to find Korean community was going to church. My grandfather attended a big Korean church where he found all of his community members. He was not as close to his aunt and didn’t want to live off of her. My grandpa decided to stay in a cheap, tiny apartment while sharing a room with 5-6 other people. The whole floor was tiles, no carpet to be seen. He endured the lack of space and the sharing of one bathroom as he knew his sacrifice would soon bring financial stability for his family in Korea. Through his Korean friends, he was able to find a job at a local Korean restaurant. My grandpa didn’t know English and he really missed Korean food. This job was going to be perfect for him! American life wasn’t easy. He didn’t face any racism while living as an immigrant, but he faced many dangerous incidents. One day he was at a gas station, filling up his car tank as usual. Then before he could even turn around, he felt a cold, firm object shoving into his side. As he slowly looked down, he realized it was a gun. The guy in dark clothing demanded money, and my grandpa had no choice but to hand it over. All his work savings for that week had been lost. Even though this incident left him quite scared and extra aware of his surroundings, my grandpa was a strong man. He didn’t let that linger in his mind for too long. In 1989, my grandma with their four children (my 3 aunts and my dad) immigrated to America. My grandmother always had discomfort in her legs since she was in Korea but after a few years, she began to lose sensation in her right leg, then her left leg, then her two arms. She was paralyzed. Even the doctors had no idea what condition she had. They told them it was a mystery. With my grandmother being paralyzed, my dad and my three aunts had to jump straight into the workforce and drop from their education. My grandfather’s wealthy aunt, who he moved to Pennsylvania for, offered my dad a job at the sock company, and he worked hard everyday. My grandfather had to work extra hard for his wife, and he was the most caring person I know. He would bring grandma a beautiful flower from the garden each morning, and he continued to work at the Korean restaurant as that way he could bring grandma Korean food. He worked long nights with rough sleep patterns so I could have bright mornings in a classroom. He couldn’t afford college. I get to write essays and complain about school, and now I will be attending a prestigious American college that I never imagined myself going to. This is not a pity story. His sacrifice wasn’t something he ever complained about. He always explained it as something that had to be done. And that’s what I admire most. His persistence and determination are the foundation I’ve built my life around. So, thank you.
