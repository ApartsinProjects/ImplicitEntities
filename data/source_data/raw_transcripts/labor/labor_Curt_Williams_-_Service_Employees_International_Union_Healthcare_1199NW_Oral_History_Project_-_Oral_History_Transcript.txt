[Ronald] Reagan got elected my sophomore year, I think, and that was a horrible moment. That’s what woke me

It just felt like the world was going in the wrong direction with Reagan, and I wanted to do something

I had friends who were familiar with ACORN and I went and interviewed with them. They had a great 
organizer who was leading their program. He taught me how to house visit and build leadership and

You had to be pretty political because you made no money. I would buy bus tokens or subway tokens and save

[00:06:32] SAI: What was the transition out of ACORN like, and what did you do next? 


[00:06:38] CURT: I moved home to Baltimore because I was out of money, and I went to work for the first

[00:07:53] SAI: How was your experience with SEIU or 1199 compared to your experience in ACORN? How

[00:08:04] CURT: It was much more about a workplace rather than a neighborhood. It was some of the same 
skills. You needed to identify leadership, you needed to ask agitational questions, you needed to be a good 
listener. There were very useful skills I learned at ACORN that were helpful with the organizing. 


The hard part was to get workers to talk to you as they’re driving out of the nursing home. Our strategies were a 
little weak in those days, so we’d stand out front and try to talk to people and meet with people, and then meet 
with them at the Denny’s later. So, it took a little while to get some campaigns going, but once we got them 
going, it was pretty lively. 


[00:08:51] SAI: What are some of the campaigns you’ve worked on that stand out the most and why? 


[00:08:58] CURT: We organized a nursing home outside of Annapolis. That was my very first campaign and I

won, for sure, but people stopped talking to me. I kept fooling myself, thinking they were for the union because

Then we ran it again because management committed to [unintelligible] and won the campaign, but never 


negotiated a first contract, which was sad. Then we had some other campaigns in Baltimore, and in Washington, 
D.C., we had a couple of big campaigns.

The biggest success I really had was organizing the social workers who worked for the City of Washington.

[00:11:19] SAI: What was your experience with the Steelworkers like, and what was your role? 


[00:11:23] CURT: I was hired to be the organizing coordinator for a district that covered Michigan and northern 
Ohio, a very industrial past. You could tell they’d had a heyday and were on the downward slope on the other 
side of it. 


We started organizing a mini-mill. We were trying to organize a mini-mill in Monroe, Michigan, which was a 
target right in their sweet spot. I got a lot of interns to come up to the organizing institutes. These were young 
students who wanted to be organizers. 


We had about 10 of them and we went and house visited all these folks from North Star Steel. We got to a bare 
majority, about 51, 52 percent. I didn’t think it was ready to go to an election but the district director, who was a 
very charismatic guy but not a terribly progressive guy, just wanted to run it anyway. Didn’t want to hear 
anything I had to say about management’s campaign or any of that, and thought he could convince people 
because of his charisma. He never really came down and did much with it, and we lost the campaign pretty bad. 


That was a tough one. Then about six months later, we had a big campaign in the western part of the state at a 
meat packing plant. We had a wonderful Latino organizer. It was a Latino workforce and then an Asian 
workforce. They were all recent immigrants. Incredibly hard work. Exploitive work. People really got injured at 
this place. 


We got a good majority, and the district director that I told you about earlier, Harry Lester, sent out some 
organizers that he hand-picked. They showed up in Lincoln Town Cars, and they didn’t really want to organize, 
and they didn’t want to work the hours, so there was some contentious back and forth with them after a couple 
of days. 


Harry called me back to Detroit and said he needed to have a one-on-one meeting with me. The long and the 
short of that meeting was that he told me that these were marginal people and we don’t want to organize 
them—it was an incredibly upsetting meeting—and he said he was going to pull the resources. 


I said, “We’re not leaving. We’re going to run this campaign.” We had an election in two weeks. I got the 


international union in Pittsburgh to send me some people. It was terrible. We lost the election by 40 votes. We 
could have won it if Harry hadn’t done this.

That was a very politicizing event for me. I could never work for him again and I left. I didn’t leave the 
Steelworkers, so they gave me a choice to move to Houston or move to Seattle. I moved to Seattle and we 
organized an aluminum smelter down in Vancouver, Washington, with organizing institute interns. 


This was the biggest organizing drive the Steelworkers had won in 20 years—570 workers or something like 
that—at [Benalco?] . I went to the convention about a month and a half after that in Pittsburgh. They tried to run 
an initiative on the convention floor that would ban organizers who came from college; that you would have to 
be hired off the shop floor in order to be hired into an organizing job for Steelworkers. 


After winning this big campaign—the biggest one they’d won in a long time—it was where this reaction to 
people like myself and this new wave of organizers—there was just a real bummer. While Lynn Williams, who 
was the president at the time, was great, and he fought it off, and was very eloquent in resisting it, it didn’t bode 
well for the future. 


We tried to organize US Air and some other things, and I was on the road all the time. I’d be on the road for 10 
days and home for two or three. I was in a new marriage and it wasn’t working out so well. We had a big 
campaign in Utah, I remember, and that was very tough. The Mormon Church got involved and people all of a 
sudden stopped talking to me. We lost that campaign. 


We had a [bike? fike?] group in Kent that we won. It was a little here, a little there. Then we had these big 
campaigns with airlines that didn’t go so well. My wife got pregnant and I didn’t want to be on the road any 
more. I was coming home. And 1199 here, I was familiar with because I’d worked for 1199 in Baltimore. I 
came to work in 1995 for 1199 here in Seattle. 


[00:16:43] SAI: You mentioned something about the Steelworkers campaign about how organizing jobs were 
coming from people who were in the shop, or how they were campaigning for that? How has your perspective 
been influenced by the fact that you had been an organizer pretty much since college? How has that perspective 
influenced your work? 


[00:17:08] CURT: It’s a really good question. I think there is an authenticity for people who’ve come out of the 
shop. For a steelworker to talk about their own experience, and that’s why they’re organizing, really makes it 
more real. I would say the same thing in healthcare. People who come out of the hospital and organize and are 
really committed to organizing are generally the best organizers. 


In some ways, I think of my job as trying to recruit people from inside the facilities to come out and organize. 
The problem a little bit with the steelworkers is that they thought of the job as their upward mobility more than 
they thought of it as a political job, where I could really change the world and impact the politics of it. 


The shop floor is not a pleasant place to be all the time, so if you want to improve your life, one of those ways is 
to get one of the jobs for the Steelworkers Union. They have a great pension, and it’s one of the jobs where you 
take a shower after work, and when you go to work for the union, you take the shower before work. [laughing] 
it’s different. 


So, they were coveted jobs, and they created a political problem for the union. I remember, they had a pecking 
order in the steel mill. You had the basic mill plants, which were huge. That was where the highest level of 
bargaining happening happened. They were shutting down a fair amount of them. 


The lowest level was the foundry, where they would melt steel from old cars and other places, or just make 
parts. Those guys were the hardest workers, the lowest paid, and always the best organizers. If I could find guys 
out of the foundry, they were the best to work with. I remember having a little crew of folks from the foundry,

but I couldn’t get the basic steel guys to really—it was hard work, and it was different than what they thought,

[00:19:34] SAI: Could you speak a little about your experience then working with the SEIU 1199? 


[00:19:40] CURT: I knew the organizing culture at SEIU 1199, and I knew the leadership here. I knew Diane 
[Sosne?] and Glen [last name?] from my time in Baltimore, so it was not a difficult transition. We were in the 
middle of a fight with Group Health at the time, so I right away got to work. 


The period in my life where I was having kids, it felt very meaningful. I got to do important organizing, but I 
wasn’t leading a ton of organizing, and I needed to have some flexibility to just help out with kids once the kids 
were born. 


I got back into it—I got back into new organizing again—and we opened up a whole new sector. All of our 
hospital organizing up till 1995 had been nurses leaving the WSNA because they thought of it as a craft union. 
The professional side of the WSNA dominated it, so supervisors and nurse managers were the leaders of the 
WSNA. The bedside nurses really wanted to have more voice, and that’s what we represented, and 5,000 nurses 
left the WSNA to do that. 


We started organizing behavioral health, which is a different kind of healthcare. These are folks who take care 
of folks with mental illness, generally poor folks with Medicaid. We organized Eastside Mental Health and 
Behavioral Health Resources in Olympia and a variety of behavioral health chapters, with the vision of trying to 
organize the whole state. 


I think we organized three to five of them, and then Glenn Goldstein, who was the founding organizer, left in 
1997 and I became the organizing director. Right after that, we had this huge campaign in Yakima. We represent 
the nurses in Yakima at the Memorial Hospital, and Providence owned the other, smaller hospital. 


We got a call saying that they wanted to organize a union there. I sent somebody out to go see if we could get a 
list and that kind of thing. He did a good job and found leaders. He started in April, and by June, we were ready 
to call committee meetings. We got all our cards signed, and the boss didn’t seem to know, and we filed to have 
an election with 70 percent to the Service and Business Office Clerical, a unit of about 520 people. 


Management went absolutely ballistic. I’d never seen an anti-union campaign like this. They hired two union 
busters from Buffalo, New York, who would have daily meetings with supervisors and managers. The managers 
and supervisors were really incentivized to have one-on-one meetings with workers and just scare them to 
death. People would stop talking to us after work. It wasn’t like they came and screamed at us, but they just 
didn’t want to talk, didn’t want to communicate, and our 70 percent was quickly slipping. 


This boss used to be in 1199 in New York and brought a lot of film and stuff from the 1199 local in New York. 
The East Coast union of service workers was African American workers, so he tried to make the union out to 
seem like an African American union to white workers in Yakima. It was a racist way to polarize against the 
union. People wouldn’t say that out loud, but I think that was going on in the background. 


People just got scared. We were going to lose this campaign. About two weeks before the end of the campaign, 
one of the main leaders, Bill Lord, got fired. Bill was just a really well-loved guy, and he wanted the union 
because he loved Providence so much, he wanted to make it better.

It had become such a bottom line kind of place where the money was more important than the care. That’s why 
he got involved, and then they fired him. I remember this meeting we had. We had an old train car that was the 
office, and on one side were the Teamsters who were organizing the warehouse workers, and on the other side 
was my team. 


We had this emergency meeting for Bill Lord. It was packed. It was hotter than it could be. We all agreed to do 
a sticker “Bring Bill Back,’ BBB. Then we needed to figure out some way to win this thing. I urged them to 
think about, how can we be the union? Not just put out a piece of paper saying we want to vote for the union, 
but how can we act like the union? 


Out of nowhere, somebody in the back said, “How about we do a bake sale?” I was like, really? A bake sale? 
How is that going to help us be the union? 


There was a woman in central supply who had cancer and wanted to get treatment, and needed to get treatment, 
at the UW. We had already had a fight getting petitions signed, and people willing to donate their vacation time 
so she could do it, and management wouldn’t let them do it. She said, “If we can raise $1,000, we can send her 
to get her chemo, and maybe even her husband.” 


Everybody loved that idea. Somebody else popped off, ““We can’t do that because they won’t let us use the 
lobby for back sales. They have a rule, no bake sales.” 


We debated that for a little while and we just decided to go ahead and do it and dare them to shut it down. 
People left that night really fired up. Man, it seemed like everybody in Yakima baked. That next morning, we 
set up three tables before security showed up, and we had a great leaflet with a picture of the woman who 
couldn’t get her chemo because the hospital wouldn’t let other people donate. 


“This is why we’re having our bake sale so she can take care of herself. If we can’t do for ourselves together, 
we've got to help each other. This is what the union is.” 


Man, we had another thing that sort of showed up. We would do cookie grams, where we would send cookies to 
people. For a dollar, you could send a cookie and a message. So, we sent people cookies with messages. Then 
people started sending management cookies with messages, telling them to do the right thing. 


Management canceled their mandatory meetings. They would have weekly mandatory meetings with movies, 
and all kinds of reasons to say, “Vote against the union.” Management canceled this, and it was three days 
before the election. 


So, we got the momentum back and won the election by 11 votes. So close. Man, that was quite a fight. 


We had a really hard time getting a contract out after that, too. We had to get a Catholic priest to come help us 
out, and push on Providence for not living up to their Catholic social values. 


Then we right away went into a strike in the West Seattle Psychiatric Hospital, where we had organized that, 
along with this behavioral health group. Management wouldn’t agree to a union shop and a contract, so we went 
out for 146 days on strike. 


That was an incredibly busy, important time in the union’s history. I helped out with the strike, but I came back 
from Yakima and we had another campaign at the Edmonds hospital, Stevens Hospital. It’s a public district 
hospital. 


We were just off to the races. This was the time when we organized easily a hospital a year, where there was 
never a slow time, where I was trying to find leads so that I didn’t have to do all the leadership work, and we

We had real success using NLRB tactics to organize. We then organized in Puyallup, the Good Samaritan

It went pretty quickly for a while, and then some workers in Spokane called me. Are there any other questions?

[00:28:57] CURT: Okay. We got a call from Spokane. They had two hospitals that were unorganized in terms of 
their service and maintenance group. We began to meet with those members. Lo and behold, in the midst of 
that, the boss announced that they were going to make a nine percent wage cut. They were really in trouble 
financially, and across the board, they were going to cut everybody’s wages nine percent. 


There has never been a gift more dramatic than that to get people to organize. [laughing] Then it really took off. 
The nurses jumped in and we got the whole hospital going on this. We had to get a whole slew of people out, 
and it was a long way from home. We had just merged with Local 6, so we had grown in membership. 


It was a long supply line, let me just put it that way, all the way to Spokane. I remember the leadership being 
really helpful about that. We even had an executive board meeting out in Spokane so that the workers could see 
how supportive our members in Seattle wanted to be. 


We ended up having the election, and we won all the units except for the big nurse group at the Deaconess 
Hospital, where we lost by 50, 60 votes, and we came back and won that in a year. So, we planted the flag in 
Spokane and got two big hospitals. 


It was quite a bit of work to get contracts, and these weren’t hospitals in good shape because they wouldn’t have 
done a nine percent cut, so we had to figure out how to win in that setting. Then, a for-profit health system came 
and bought them, so that complicated everything again. It was quite a bargaining challenge, and I was really 
grateful for the bargaining side of things. 


We had a really good run of organizing, from Yakima to Seattle to Puyallup, hospitals as well as behavioral 
health, and then Spokane. Our biggest nemesis has been Providence. The international asked me to go organize 
in Portland. They invested a lot of money in creating a campaign in that Local 49 to see if we could organize 
Providence, which had a huge footprint in Portland, and was unorganized. 


I worked on that for three years, commuting every week to go down there. It was quite a challenge. We got a lot 
of workers involved and had a good campaign, but Providence would not make it easy for us to organize, and 
we knew that if we didn’t have a really strong majority, we couldn’t win here. 


We never got to a strong majority. We tried to get Providence to understand that there would be a community 
campaign to get neutrality. We got a lot of priests and church folks involved and had them march on Providence. 
There was a big, huge march that happened in November to try to get Providence to do the right thing and we 
just could never get them to do it. They’re just so wealthy and so stubborn, it was incredibly difficult. 


After three years, that campaign went to Meg Niemi, who is the current president down in Local 49. I came 
back. 


[00:33:07] SAI: With all of your diverse experiences in organizing across several unions and other 
organizations, how have your strategies and your perception of organizing shifted over the years? 


[00:33:25] CURT: Organizing always happens in opposition to something. People need to be upset and have 
energy around making change. At the same time that the labor movement is being attacked now—we just had 


SEIU HEALTHCARE 1199 NW ORAL HISTORY PROJECT | CURT WILLIAMS OF SEIU HEALTHCARE 1199NW 


this recent Supreme Court Janus [v AFSCME] decision—I think that it may be one of the ripest times to try to 
organize because of the money [meeting? leaving?] the system. And the negativity, the bad impact, always goes 
downhill, so it goes to the workers, it doesn’t generally go to management. 


I’m really proud that our union is staying in, organizing, and trying to figure out how we organize in a difficult 
moment where a lot of our attention could go to sustaining our union here. But we have really good leaders that 
are in our institutions. 


There’s new advances in how quickly we can get cards signed, because we now have electronic cards and that 
kind of thing. But a union is really about building a network of trust in a workplace, and I don’t really think 
there’s a shortcut around that. You really have to build relationships, and you have to build trust, and you have 
to go through something hard. It doesn’t happen easily without some struggle. 


We just had an election in Cadillac a year ago that Theresa ran and it was a lot of the same dynamics as what we 
did in Yakima and what we’ve done in Spokane and what happened in other places. It happened quicker in some 
ways because of electronic cards and some of the other things that happened, but the dynamics are the same. 


Part of the gift is that hospitals are a space where there’s a lot of people in a confined space, so it’s much easier 
to create a network when you see the same people every day and you have a community already established, to 
some extent. 


When we have workplaces like Uber or these technology places, or even clinics, you don’t have that many 
people in one space. Everybody’s spread out, and electronic communication doesn’t lend itself to building trust. 
It’s not unhelpful, but it’s incredibly neutral. When you’re having a face-to-face conversation, you can express a 
lot more, and you can really gauge whether somebody’s with you or not. 


I think organizing is going to have to change to be in the world that we’re currently in. It’s exciting what’s 
happening with these red states and teachers. A lot of me wonders whether what’s happening with teachers is 
also happening with healthcare workers. These are all implications of red states not providing money for social 
services, for healthcare, for teaching—education. 


Hospitals may be as hard hit. They just don’t have a union as ubiquitous as the teachers union, so we need to 
somehow be out there in a way where they feel like they have a choice. I’m excited, and I think that’s a 
possibility. 


[00:37:08] SAI: Now that you’re transitioning out of SEIU 1199NW, what do you feel with your culminating

[00:37:37] CURT: I think my most important contribution was working for the organizing institute and working

teachers union in Chicago that’s doing really important work right now, Jesse Sharkey, worked with me on that

Mike Sukal, who’s the director of organizing at AFSCME, worked with me at the steelworkers for a long time.
