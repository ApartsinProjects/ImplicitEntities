President’s  Message 


This  past  year  THD  has  had  many  successes  in  zoning  issues,  as  well  as  some  losses.  We’ve 
“won”  an  extension  of  the  moratorium  on  financial  institutions  for  another  year  or  so,  but  we’ve 
“lost”  a  few  more  sites  to  new  financial  institutions  on  the  outskirts  of  our  moratorium  area.  Two 
of  these  losses  occurred  when  we  were  not  represented  at  hearings.  The  Board  sent  letters,  but 
letters  are  not  enough.  Why  weren’t  we  there?  Out  of  550  members  it  seems  someone 
i^pies  a  small  loyal,  over- worked  groups  of  people  would  be  able  to  attend  a  meeting  and  speak 
on  an  issue  that  so  affects  our  neighborhood. 

In  any  one  month,  there  are  easily  6-8  zoning  issues  going  on  at  once  in  the  North  Beach/ 
Telegraph  Hill/Waterfront  area.  Some  of  these  issues  are  watched  for  months  before  action  is 
needed.  Others  happen  quickly.  With  the  help  of  our  members  and  the  assistance  of  the  City 
Planning  Department  and  the  Board  of  Supervisors,  we  learn  of  possible  problems,  and  we  can 
act  through  phone  calls,  letters,  and  public  appearances  at  City  Hall. 

But  we  need  help!  Following  one  issue  through  the  planning  and  zoning  process  can  be 
time-consuming.  Call  me  and  tell  me  you’re  willing  to  follow  the  next  request  for  a  conditional 
use  or  variance  that  comes  up.  We  will  help  you  through  the  process.  We’ve  accumulated  the 
expertise;  now  we  need  the  support.  If  you  help,  you’ll  experience  the  great  satisfaction  of 
making  your  neighborhood  more  liveable.  Please  call  me  at  421-1466  and  volunteer  today.

Coit  Tower 

It  has  been  suggested  that  the  statue  of  Col¬ 
umbus  in  the  parking  lot  at  Coit  Tower  be 
encircled  with  an  18”  iron  fence.  The  idea  is 
to  protect  the  landscaping  around  the  bottom 
of  the  statue.  Cost  of  such  a  fence  would  be 
five  to  seven  thousand  dollars.  Anyone 
wishing  to  see  this  project  undertaken  or 
with  knowledge  about  funding  sources 
should  contact  John  Huttinger,  Park  &  Rec, 
McLaren  Lodge,  Golden  Gate  Park. 

Kearny  St.  Greening 

The  Dwellers  have  been  trying  for  years  to 
improve  the  Kearny  Street  Steps,  (between 
Broadway/V allejo)  to  remove  the  grime,  the 
broken  paving,  the  blight.  Small  trees  ^fca

History  of  THD 

( continued  from  the  last  Semaphore,  p.  1 7) 


Telegraph  Hill  Dwellers  consider  V 
ton  Square  a  very  important  park, 
businesses  in  the  area  persuaded  the  city  to 
build  a  parking  garage  under  the  square. 
Having  seen  the  effect  of  such  garages  on 
Portsmouth  Square  and  Union  Square — dep¬ 
rived  of  contours,  walled  off  from  the  sur¬ 
rounding  streets,  and  lacking  major  trees 
and  natural  beauty — the  Telegraph  Hill 
Dwellers  adamantly  opposed  the  plan  and 
were  able  to  persuade  the  mayor  to  veto  the 
garage.  Fortunately  for  the  residents’  point 
of  view,  construction  costs  for  underground 
garages  no  longer  make  them  feasible. 

The  list  of  battles  goes  on  and  on — every 
development  on  the  city’s  northern  water¬ 
front  is  scrutinized  by  volunteers  from  THD. 
THD  helped  establish  a  lower  height  limit 
between  the  hill  and  the  Embarcadero  and 
supported  the  reduction  of  the  height  in  the 
Golden  Gateway,  Phase  III.  By  blocking  the 
elevated  Embarcadero  Freeway  from  encirc¬ 
ling  the  northern  waterfront,  the  THD 
helped  start  a  nationwide  revolt  against 
urban  freeways. 

Telegraph  Hill  Dwellers  are  active^fcLn- 
volved  in  improving  their  immediafflWn- 
vironment.  Inspired  by  the  examples  set  by 
the  late  Dorothy  Erskine,  who  planted  trees 
and  improved  our  parks,  we  are  working 
closely  with  the  Friends  of  the  Urban  Forest 
to  preserve  and  extend  the  ever  decreasing 
population  of  sound-buffering,  shade¬ 
providing  sidewalk  trees.  Another  long¬ 
standing  THD  member,  Grace  Marchant, 
who  died  at  96  last  year,  was  responsible  for 
creating  the  beautiful  Filbert  Steps  area  on 
the  East  side  of  the  hill  and  is  being  remem- 


6 


bered  by  THD’s  active  sponsorship  and  en¬ 
couragement  of  beautification  projects  in  our 
area. 

In  the  last  twenty-nine  years,  the  issues  of 
■icern  to  the  Telegraph  Hill  Dwellers  have 
remained  quite  constant — parks,  cars,  zon¬ 
ing,  height  limits,  the  #39  bus,  preservation 
of  the  neighborhood.  Many  people  have  come 
and  gone  (some  stalwarts  have  stayed  with  it 
all  the  way  through),  but  the  over  500  dues- 
paying  members  in  1983  are  just  as  persis¬ 
tent  as  the  50  original  founders  were  in  1954. 

All  this  activity  over  the  years  has  been  en¬ 
hanced  by  a  variety  of  spectacular  social  acti¬ 
vities — from  the  formal  25th  anniversary 
dinner  dance  at  the  Italian  American  Athle¬ 
tic  Club  in  1979  to  annual  new  member  cock¬ 
tail  parties.  For  the  past  two  years,  the  ele¬ 
gant  Christmas  Social  has  been  hosted  by 
Julius’  Castle.  Our  group’s  quarterly  dinner 
meetings  have  been  held  at  such  North 
Beach  landmarks  as  Caesar’s,  San  Remo, 
Des  Alpes  and  the  Old  Spaghetti  Factory  res¬ 
taurants,  featuring  not  only  fine  food,  but 
also  an  open  forum  for  participating  in  THD 
policy.  Representatives  from  various  City 
Hall  departments  are  often  present  to  discuss 
issues  of  interest  and  concern  to  the  mem¬ 
bership  at  large.

Public  Service  Guide 


Reprinted  from  Assemblyman  Art  Agnos’s 
newsletter 

Rental  Agreements 

To  protect  the  rights  of  both  property  owners 
and  renters  all  rental  agreements  should  be 
made  in  writing.  By  law,  a  lease  for  a  period 
longer  than  one  year  must  be  in  writing. 

Cleaning  or  Security  Deposits 

Property  owner  may  keep  that  part  of  the 
deposit  needed  to  pay  for  unpaid  rent,  to  re¬ 
pair  damages  to  the  apartment  caused  by  the 
renter,  (beyond  normal  wear  and  tear),  and 
to  clean  the  apartment.  The  refund  should  be 
returned  to  the  tenant  within  two  weeks  af- 
ter  moving  out.  The  renter  cannot  use  secur¬ 
ity  deposits  in  place  of  the  last  month’s  rent. 

•  Since  September  1,  1983  city  law  has  re¬ 
quired  property  owners  to  pay  interest  on 
any  security  deposits  they  have  held.  If  a 
renter  resides  in  an  apartment  for  more  than 
one  year  the  property  owner  must  pay  the 
renter  annual  interest  of  5%  on  any  deposits. 

Rent  Increases 

After  March  1  of  each  year,  property  owners 
may  increase  rents  by  between  4%  and  7%. 
The  actual  percentage  allowed  is  set  by  the 
Residential  Rent  Stabilization  and  Arbitra¬ 
tion  Board  at  the  beginning  of  each  year  and 
is  based  on  the  rate  of  inflation  in  the  preced¬ 
ing  year. 

For  the  period  from  March  1,  1984  to  Febru¬ 
ary  28,  1985  property  owners  may  increase 


rents  by  as  much  as  4%.  The  increase  must  be 
based  on  a  renter’s  current  rent  and  may  not 
include  any  extra  charges  such  as  utility  pas¬ 
sthroughs.  Rent  cannot  be  increased  unless* 
one  full  year  has  passed  since  the  last  in™ 
crease. 

Property  owners  also  may  increase  rent  each 
year  to  cover  the  costs  of  capital  improve¬ 
ments,  building  rehabilitation,  energy  con¬ 
servation  measures,  or  increased  utility 
costs.  Most  of  these  increases  must  be 
approved  in  advance  by  the  Rent  Board. 
Property  owners:  remember  to  check  with 
the  Rent  Board  before  increasing  rents. 

If  a  property  owner  decreases  services  this 
may  be  considered  a  rent  increase  under  the 
law.  For  example,  if  a  property  owner  takes 
away  the  use  of  a  garage  which  was  previous¬ 
ly  made  available,  this  could  be  considered  a 
rent  increase. 

Finally,  with  any  rent  increase,  property 
owners  must  notify  tenants  in  writing  at 
least  thirty  days  before  the  date  the  rent  in¬ 
crease  takes  effect.  This  notice  must  indicate 
what  each  part  of  the  increase  reflects:  the 
annual  increase,  costs  for  capital  improve¬ 
ments,  etc. 


Repairs 


Unless  the  renter  causes  damage,  the  proper¬ 
ty  owner  must  make  sure  that  the  apartment 
meets  these  minimum  standards: 


•  no  leaks,  no  broken  doors  or  windows; 

•  the  plumbing  must  work  including  hot  and 
cold  water  and  working  sewer  or  septic 
tank  connection; 


•  the  heater  must  be  available  a  minimum  of 
thirteen  hours  per  day  in  residential  hotels 
and  apartments; 

•  the  lights  and  wiring  must  work  and  be 
safe; 


12 


floors,  stairways  and  railings  must  be  in 
good  repair; 

must  be  clean  when  rented; 

ve  sufficient  garbage  cans  with  covers; 
rats,  mice,  roaches,  or  other  pests. 

A  renter  should  notify  a  property  owner  in 
writing  of  any  needed  repairs  and  keep  a 
copy. 

Under  state  law,  if  the  repairs  are  not  made 
by  the  property  owner  within  a  reasonable 
time,  the  renter  can  make  the  repairs  when 
the  cost  of  the  repairs  is  not  more  than  one 
month’s  rent.  The  tenant  must  keep  a  receipt 
of  the  costs  and  can  deduct  them  from  the 
next  rent  payment.  This  right  can  only  be 
used  once  in  an  12-month  period. 

The  Rent  Board  can  deny  a  property  owner 
any  part  of  the  allowable  annual  rent  in¬ 
crease  until  the  necessary  repairs  are  made. 

Moving  Out/Evictions 

Renters  normally  must  give  30  days  written 
notice  of  their  plans  to  leave.  Without  proper 
notice,  the  renter  may  have  to  pay  additional 
rent.  In  a  month-to-month  rental  the  proper¬ 
ty  owner  must  give  thirty  days  written  notice 
asking  the  renter  to  leave.  If  the  renter  is 
bsMnd  in  the  rent,  the  property  owner  must 
days  written  notice  asking  the  renter 
to  pay  or  move  out. 

A  property  owner  cannot  legally  walk  into  an 
apartment  and  physically  remove  or  lock  out 
a  renter  or  turn  off  utilities.  Evictions  must 
be  done  through  the  courts.  A  property  owner 
must  have  a  “just  cause”  to  evict,  such  as 
failure  to  pay  rent  or  violation  of  the  terms  of 
a  rental  agreement.  If  a  renter  believes  he 
has  been  evicted  without  just  cause  he  may 
petition  the  Rent  Board  to  have  the  eviction 
reviewed. 


Discrimination 

Discrimination  in  housing  on  the  basis  of 
race,  color,  religion,  national  origin,  ances¬ 
try,  sex  and  marital  status,  as  well  as  against 
blind,  visually  handicapped,  physically  dis¬ 
abled,  or  families  with  children,  is  illegal.  If 
you  believe  a  property  owner  is  discriminat¬ 
ing  against  you,  contact  the  San  Francisco 
Human  Rights  Commission  or  The  Califor¬ 
nia  Department  of  Fair  Employment  and 
Housing.

Department  of  Consumer  Affairs.  It  is  current 
as  of  March,  1984.

Port  Update 

Last  Fall,  San  Francisco  lost  an  outstanding 
Port  Director  and  we  lost  a  good  friend,  Ed 
David.  He  worked  hard  and  successfully  to 
revive  our  ailing  port.  Members  of  the  THD 
Waterfront  Committee  had  many  occasions 
to  work  closely  with  him  on  important  water¬ 
front  issues  and  admired  him  for  his  integri¬ 
ty  and  human  warmth.  His  vision  extended 
beyond  strictly  maritime  responsibilities  to 
include  the  vital  role  the  waterfront  has 
played  for  the  entire  city  since  its  beginning.

Former  Port  Commission  President  Eugene 
L.  Gartland  was  appointed  to  succeed  David. 
In  a  speech  to  our  members  last  February, 
new  Director  offered  a  dynamic  preview 
is  plans,  which  include  securing  an  effi¬ 
cient  railroad  link  for  the  port.  Recent  press 
reports  indicate  that  this  goal  is  close  to  a 
realization.  Like  his  predecessor,  Eugene 
Gartland  stresses  the  waterfront’s  crucial 
role  in  the  city’s  history — past  and  future. 
We  are  looking  forward  to  continued  coopera¬

appointment  as  a  Port  Commissioner!  We 
also  congratulate  the  Port  Commission  for

Pfeiffer  Park  Under  Way! 

Work  on  this  park,  designed  for  the  stub  end 
of  Pfeiffer  Street  above  Grant,  will  begin 
soon! 


After  hearings  before  the  Department  of 
Public  Works  (3/16)  and  the  Supervisors’ 
Public  Works  Committee  (4/13),  at  which 
legal  challenges  to  the  city’s  title  to  the  street 
and  its  right  to  allow  the  park  were  dismis¬ 
sed,  the  Board  of  Supervisors  approved  the 
permit  unanimously  on  April  23rd. 

The  park,  to  be  built  and  maintained  by  the 
developer,  at  no  cost  to  the  city,  will  greatly 
enhance  the  quality  of  life  in  our  crowded 
neighborhood.  No  longer  will  the  stub  end  of 
Pfeiffer  Street  be  covered  with  impenetrable 
underbrush — a  street  belonging  to  all  but 
accessible  to  no  one.  The  park’s  design  was 
worked  out  over  two  years  in  lengthy  ses¬ 
sions  between  the  developer’s  landscape 
architect  and  the  neighborhood  advisory 
committee.  Its  informal  scheme  consists  of  a 
meandering  stair  at  grade,  constructed  of 
railroad  ties,  and  exposed-aggregate  land¬ 
ings  leading  to  a  small  overlook  whose  loca-  i 
tion  will  be  determined  when  work  reaches 
the  top  of  Pfeiffer  Street.  Planting  will  in¬ 
corporate  existing  trees,  additional  native 
trees,  shrubs,  groundcover,  and  a  sprinkler 
system. 

The  project  actually  started  back  in  1962, 
when  Jack  Early  obtained  a  grant  to  plant 
trees  and  beautify  the  bottom  portion  of 
Pfeiffer  above  Grant.  Our  thanks  go  to  Jack 
who  has  faithfully  maintained  the  trees,  the 
landscape  architect  and  the  design  commit¬ 
tee  who  worked  out  a  sensitive  design,  the 
city  agencies  who  expedited  the  permit  proc¬ 
edure  and  the  Board  of  Supervisors  for  their 
unanimous  approval.  Special  thanks  to  Su¬ 
pervisors  Maher  and  Hongisto  who  were  able 
to  untangle  some  last-minute  legal  objec¬ 
tions  that  threatened  to  delay  the  park  con¬ 
struction.
