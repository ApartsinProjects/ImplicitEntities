============================================================

﻿Da Poe


Interviewed by Irene Hsu, done with an interpreter
Transcribed by Claire Schmeller, June 20th, 2020
(note: a lot was spoken out of English between Da Poe and interpreter)








(00:01) Irene: Can you tell me your name and where you're from?


(00:05) Da Poe: Da Poe. My name is Da Poe. I'm from Burma and Thailand.


(00:19) Irene: Can you tell me a little bit about the place you're from?


(00:26) Da Poe: Came from Thailand. Came from Burma. 


(00:56) Interpreter: First, when she first came from Burma, it was hard. The Burmese were attacking the place where she was living. When they were farming, the Burmese came and burned all of it. Then, they didn't have food so they came to America. The Americans offered for them to come and then they came. 


(01:59) Irene: What did you do in Burma before you came?


(02:12) Interpreter: Farming, making money, and helping out.


(02:19) Irene: So you were in Burma, and then you went to Thailand?


(02:50) Interpreter: The place where she was living, the rules and laws were hard to live within so they moved to Thailand. 


(03:04) Irene: Tell me about the journey from Burma to Thailand.


(03:44) Interpreter: There was a lot of war.


(03:47) Irene: Did you walk there? Or how did you get to Thailand?


(04:26) Interpreter:All on foot.
(04:41) Irene: What was it like living in the refugee camp in Thailand?


(04:51) Interpreter: There wasn't any in Thailand. She came to a refugee camp in America.


(05:07) Irene: So she came directly?


(05:09) Interpreter: Mhm.


(05:09) Irene: Okay. What was something that surprised you about America?


(05:53) Interpreter: Everything is new. Back then, there wasn't really anything. You see a lot more new things like cars and different places. Planes and stuff. 


(06:46) Interpreter: She is saying, Thank God, for everything that has happened.


(06:54) Irene: What did you like the most about coming here?


(07:20) Interpreter: She liked everything about it, because back then, in the place where she came from, everything was really small, like the buildings and stuff, and then she came to America and she looked at everything, and it was big.
The different celebrations and stuff; holidays. And where she came from, there was none of that.


(08:17)  Interpreter: When she came here, her whole family had to work and get money to survive and it was really easy for them, because it was really fun.


(08:45) Irene: How many years have you been in the states?


(08:50) Da Poe: 10 years.


(08:51) Irene: 10 years? Wow.


(08:58) Interpreter: Came here in 2009.


(09:08) Irene: Do you miss, or what do you miss about your home country?


(09:20) Interpreter: Her family, sisters and brother.


(09:27) Irene: Is a lot of your family still in Burma?


(09:33) Da Poe:  5. One brother and four sisters.


(09:43) Irene: Do you stay in contact with them?


(09:44) Da Poe: Yeah.


(09:48) Irene: How do you do that? By phone or do you get to see them?
(09:50) Interpreter: Phone.


(09:55) Irene: Have you ever been back?


(09:59) Da Poe: Yes, two times.


(10:05) Irene: What was that like?


(10:08) Interpreter: She didn't like it, it was not the same as America.


(10:15) Irene: It is not the same as here?


(10:16) Interpreter: Yes.


(10:19) Irene: How was it different?


(10:25) Interpreter: There is a very big difference. It is different because there is a lot of grass here and trees, and there isn't very much there.  The roads are very different, dust comes up when you drive. The restrooms weren't good. It is kind of getting better, she said.


(11:28) Irene: What would you want people to know about refugees?


(11:56) Interpreter: She doesn't know how to explain it. 


(12:01) Irene: Give like small bits and parts.


(12:23) Interpreter: She said, when she came to America, they gave her an education to go to school. Back then, she didn't have that education.


(12:39) Irene: So you're very grateful?


(12:51) Interpreter: Very much.


(12:54) Irene: What was it like raising a family here in the States?


(13:08) Interpreter: It is a very good place. The conditions and the laws, she really likes.


(13:19) Irene: Did you have all your kids when you were here or did you have your kids and then bring them with you?


(13:39) Interpreter: All of them were in Thailand. 


(13:50) Irene: Was it nice to raise your family here?


(14:03) IntInterpretererpreter: Yes. If they're respectful.


(14:13) Irene: Do you have anything that you want to share about your experience and your country and any last thoughts?


(14:57) Interpreter: The experience she had is very welcoming. In America, you can do mostly everything freely. Back then, there were no restrictions. If you wanted to drive, you can drive. She learned to drive here. In Thailand, she didn't get to drive.


(15:56) Da Poe: God bless you.


(16:03) Interpreter: She basically said, she is blessed to have everything.


(16:15) Irene: Any last thoughts?


(16:37) Interpreter: She said, thanks America for allowing her to come here and have a new life. 


(17:01) Irene: Thank you so much. I really enjoyed listening to you.
