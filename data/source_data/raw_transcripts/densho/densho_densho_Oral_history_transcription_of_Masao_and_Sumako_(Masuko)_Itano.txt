Q; Where were you born? 


A: Okayama Prefecture ahout 50 miles from Okayama City. 


Q: Did you go to school after you came to America? 

A: Yes, I did. I worked for a while as a houseboy 
to earn some capital to attend school. I entered 
Tamalpias High School in Mills Valley in Marin County. 
In 1913, I graduated from the high school and entered 
the University of California and graduated in 1917. 
In those days, students were few at the University 
of California and there were only 40 to 50 in the 


graduating class. 


Q: How old were you when you graduated from high school? 
A: I was 28 years old when I graduated from the Univer- 
sity and finished high school 4 years earlier. You 
see, I graduated in 1917, and I was born in 1889. 
I was the only Japanese in the graduating class of the 


high school, too. 


Q: Did you come here to attend school? 
A: Yes. I wanted to enter school to study. In fact, 
when I left Japan, my plan was to return in 10 years 


after finishing college. 


Q: Did you come to Marin County first? 


A: I landed in Seattle and came down to San Francisco but 


stayed there for 3 or 4 months. Moved to Mills Valley 
after I found a houseboy job and saved a little money. 
It was a few years afterword that I entered high school. 
I came over in 1906 entered high school in 1910, 
graduated from high school in 1913 and from the 


university in 1917. 


Q: What was your major in College? 

A: Iwas an agriculture major. Those days, there 
wasn't any job for a Japanese graduating from the 
University, but I was planning to start farming. I 
went back to Japan to look for a wife, after coming 


back with a wife, I started farming in Freeport Just 


opposite of today's Sacramento Executive Airport. 


Q: Did you ever think of getting employment in some 
company? 

A: No. I knew then that a job was hard to come by for 
a Japanese. If one wanted to work for a Japanese, 
there were jobs such as a fruit stand sttendant. 
There was a person who majored in commercial subjects 
and got a job at the Sumitomo Bank in San Francisco 
for low wages. There were very few that had jobs 
in those days because jobs were scarce. There were 


more that went back to Japan after finishing school 


due to the lack of employment. 


What kind of farming did you do in Freeport? 

With two partners, we leased three hundred acres 

of land for $10,000 to raise ,but my partners 
and I were so inexperienced that the venture was 

a total failure. After this sad experience, we 

came out to Sacramento to live. I did some interpre- 
ting work and some odd jobs to make a living for about 
3 years. Then I went into the insurance business 

til] the out break of the war, at which time I had 

to give it up because I was interned, first in Bismarck, 
North Dakota, then to Roseburng and Santa Fe, New 


Mexico. 


Those were special camps, weren't they? 

They were internment camps and different from the 
relocation camps where everyone else was sent. Those 
who were considered to be.leaders of the Japanese 
community and dangerous enemy aliens were sent to 
internment camp. It wasn't like a jail but ; 
For one night I was detained in the City Jail ,)orn 
February 16,)'1942..° In this town): there were: chree 
persons who got picked up the day the war broke out. 
One of them was Mr. Giichi Aoki and another was Mr. 
Rikitaro Sato, who was then the president of the Japanese 
Association and Jukichi Miyakawa, local manager of 
the Nichi Bei Shinbun (newspaper). I didn't get 


picked up until February 16 of the following year 


along with about 10 others. We stayed in town one 


night and were moved to Silver Avenue, in San Francisco, 
where the immigration office was. Here we got together 
with those who were arrested in other communities 

and finally shipped out to Bismarck, North Dakota, 

by train. After a month's stay there, we were sent 

to Roseburg and then to SantaFe, New Mexico. After 
getting paroled from the interment camp, I joined 

my family at Tule Lake RElocation Center on July 4, 
1943. We were separated from each other for about 


a year and a half.

have left the camp.
