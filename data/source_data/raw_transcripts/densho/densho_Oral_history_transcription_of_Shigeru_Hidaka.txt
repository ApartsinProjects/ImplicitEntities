Questionnaire 
(Mr. Shigeru Hidaka) 
I. In Japan 


A. Where were you born? I was born in Shimane Ken, Japan. 

B. When were you born? My birthday is May 28, 1904. 

C. Do you remember anything about wars? Yes 
Sino-Japanese War? I was not born then. 


Russo-Japanese war? As 1904 being Meiji (37th year of Meiji) it 
seems my birthday happened at the time when this war was going on. 


World War 1? I came to the United States during World War I - 1918, 


Manchurian event? Anti-Japanese feeling among the whites and Chinese 
in Seattle became very intense as I recall. 


D. Do you remember any events like tsunami, Earthquake, plagues? When 
I was three years old, father immigrated to the U.S., leaving mother 
and I behind. For 11 years we lived separately. 


How about happy events, sad events, things you suffered? Saddest 
experience was when my whole face and hands were burned in an 
accident. Luckily, though, the burned parts were healed completely. 
All I know about Kanto earthquake was what I read in papers then, 


E. Religion of family? My religion in Japan was Buddhism(Jodo Shisu Sect). 


F. Did you hear anything about Christianity in Japan? I: had-not known 
of Christianity at all when I was in Japan. 


G. How was the condition of your family when you left Japan? Mother and 
I accompanied by father immigrated to U.S. in 1918. Father visited 
homeland then for the purpose of bringing his family to this country. 


Society? About society at the time--as it was war-time Seattle was 
boom town, prosperous, gay and noisy where we used to live namely 
the International district in Seattle, 


H. How much formal education did you have? As I had to leave Japan when 
I was fourteen years old, my education in Japan was limited. I received 
Certificate of Completion of Koto-ka or eighth grade grammar school. 


I. What was the reason why you came over to the USA? There was no specific 
desire or purpose in my mind when I came to the United States.

On the Ship 


The name of the ship that we came on was S.S. Canada Maru of Osaka Shosen, 
The trip was uneventful until the ship reached near the mouth of Juan de 
Fuca. Canada Maru became agrounded at Cape Flattery. We were rescued and 
brought over to Canada and to U.S. by S.S. Tatsuta Maru of Nippon Yusen 
Kaisha, 


A. When did you leave Japan? Canada Maru Sailed Japan on July 17, 1918. 
Tatsuta brought us to U.S. August 4, behind schedule by 3 days. 


B. Did you go through Hawaii? No. 


C. What kind of people were on the Ship? Seemed to be all immigrants 
such as I. Many photo brides and kids. 


D. What kind of ship? Canada Maru was 9000 ton class passenger-freighter 
type of steamship. 


E. Do you remember anything which happened on the ship? It is mentioned 
above, 


F. What was your expectation of America? I thought America was beautiful 
country. My secret ambition was to become an artist. 


First Impressions of America 


A. What was your first impression of America? "America IS beautiful!" 
That was my first impression. 


B. How did immigration officers treat you? I spent three days in Immigra- 
tion Detention Building in Victoria, B.C. after ship mishap. I spent 
one day at Seattle Station. Nothing special to mention. 


C. What did you do when you landed in America for the first time? Jobs? 
Arriving Seattle in August, come September I entered special foreigners 
class in Main Street School, starting from ABC. 


D. How did white people treat you? After six months, I took a school 
boy job in Alpha Delta Pi Sorority house at University of Washington. 
Nineteen girls there and they treated me well. This was my first 
real contact with Americans. 


E. Memories concerning various situations--happy experiences, sad experi- 
ences, funny ones, bad ones, frightening ones, helpless ones...? 


None of these various experiences that I can recall. 


Settlement 


A. How did you find your bride? With the assistance of "baishaku nin", 
on September 19, 1936, I got married. 


oh 


What kind ot wedding ceremony did you have? Where? Our marriage 
ceremony was performed by Rev. Eiji Kawamorita, a Presbyterian 
minister, and at the home of the bride. 


Have you ever been home sick? No. 


What kind of hardship did you have in order to get used to the 
American way of life? None. 


Have you ever been discriminated? have you been physically harmed? 
No. 


How did you learn English? I finished grammar school in four years, 
and during most of the time I lived in American home as a school boy. 
Living with Americans helped me to learn English quicker than living 
at home with the folks. And, I took several Correspondence Courses 

in my twenties, such courses as: Federal School of Commercial Art, 
Sherwin Cody of English, Pelman Institute of Scientific Mind Training, 
etc. These courses, while trying to understand text books, I had 
acquired great deal of knowledge of English. 


How much income did you have? We've had none, 


When did you buy house? how much? where? Did you have any difficulties 
in buying it? Did any one assist you? I bought a house in 1953 after 
I became citizen, then when daughter married I gave it to her. I have 
bought apartment houses that I now own in January, 1963. My wife and 
I manage both apartment houses. No one had assisted us. But in the 
beginning of my business career, back in 1948, after a long duration 
of illness with TB, I leased a hotel with help of a friend and my 
Parents, Bought a house later but sold six years later...that is our 
daughter sold it to buy another house, 


Did you buy land? Yes. When I say ''yes" here, I mean that I bought 
apartment houses with the ground underneath of them also. 


Japanese organizations like JACL, kenjinkai, churches, Japanese council, 
farm organizations, helped you in any way? No. 


What kind of entertainment, hobbies, and other things did you enjoy? 
Driving, photography, outing to mountains, mushroom hunting in the fall. 


What do you remember about rearing children? I have a daughter happily 
married. Aside from my wife's and my wish and hope that our daughter 
to become a good mother to her children and good wife to her husband, 
my wife especially had put her heart and soul into our daughter's 
musical education, namely piano playing. It is our profound satisfac- 
tion that our daughter had tried her utmost whereby she now serves as 
one of organists at church. 


Do you remember anything about segregated schools? No.

What kind of contributions did you make during the World War 1? 

I have been for the past ten years a board member and chairman 

of the maintenance department of Japanese Language School of Seattle. 
There definitely is an urgent need for our grandchildren to know 
Japanese language. But how to get them to study purposefully is our 
agony here at the school, 


When did you come in contact with Christianity? I came to realize 
the existence of God through suffering with TB. 


Depression Period 


A. 


What kind of hardship did you experience? In 1931, father and I lost 
job on same day. No job was to be had. We acquired a small cafe. 

We worked night and day to make the ends meet as profit margins were 
so small as meal prices had to be kept very low. People had no money. 


When did you decide to stay here permanently? I have had no desire 
to return to Japan at any time, I firmed my conviction to become 
soil of this country after I had been given Citizenship in 1953. 


World War II 


What kind of attitude did whites have towards Japanese? January 12, 
1942 to February, 1946, I had been confined at Firland TB Sanatorium. 
Aithough I was an enemy alien, no one treated me badly at any time, 
Equal treatment that I had received there confirmed my belief that 
this country is basically good country! 


When Japan attacked Pearl Harbor, what did you think first of all? 
How did you feel? At that fateful hour of bombing of Pearl Harbor, 
I have been stricken with massive hemorrhages. With the declaration 
of war, myself bedridden, I thought my fate was doomed. Agonizing 
days! . 


How did you come to know the Notice of relocation? What were you 
doing at that time? While my parents, brother, sister and my family 
were sent to Minidoka Relocation Camp, I remained at Firland San 
until the end of the war. 


How did you take care of your house, possessions and business? 
My wife told me that she had our belongings stored in one of the 
warehouses of War Relocations Authority. 


How old were your children when you evacuated? My daughter was 
five years old at the time. 


How did white churches react to this evacuation? I don't know 
as I was not yet a Christian.

D. What kind of events took place there? I am not qualified to answer

VIII. Resettlement--Reconstruction 


C. Where did you go first? My wife and daughter returned to Seattle 
in August 1945. I was still at the Sanatorium. Until such time 
as I'd be discharged, my family rented a cabin belonging to 
Director of American Friends Service. 


D. Why did you go there? Upon my discharge from the San in February, 
1946, through the help of State Rehabilitation, we rented an apart- 
ment in City housing project. We became recipient of Washington 
State Welfare assistance. State helped three of us every month 
with $93 till the day that I became physically strong enough to 
seek employment. But December, 1948, with the help (financial) 

I had leased a hotel downtown as I've mentioned. 


E. What kind of job did you have? After getting into hotel business 
and later apartment operations, I became selfemployed. 


H. How did Japanese churches reconstruct themselves? The Christian 
Churches in Seattle. After the war six Protestant churches namely-- 
Japanese Baptist, Blaine Memorial Methodist, St. Peters Episcopal, 
Japanese Congregational, Holiness, and Japanese Presbyterian Church 
formed Federation having a combined Sunday services at Baptist 
Church. Later, circumstances had forced these denominations return 
to pre-war church buildings. However, once each month or special 
occasions they have combined services. Ecumenical you might say. 


I. How was the attitude of whites towards you after the war? It has 
steadily improved. 


J. How was the process of education of your children? It would be 
of utmost importance that the tradition that had been set by the 
Issei as regards to education of their children must be pursued 
by each succeeding generation. To the future generations of 
Japanese Americans as in the past generation, they must have an 
equal or better education than so called white Americans. 


K. How did you find house? job? who helped you to find them? Answer 
already have been given on this subject. 


L. What was the most difficult experience you had during this time? 
This question, I assume, is rather directed toward a returnee from 
a relocation camp. My case as you can see is quite different. 
After my discharge from the San. February, 1946, my wife and 
little daughter had been living in a rented cabin owned by Director 
of Regional American Friends Service, Mr. Floyd Schmoe at north end 


of Seattle. As I previously stated Washington State Rehabilitation

We were on relief! Health under par and no money in pocket, not

Appendix 


A. 


As a Christian what is your hope for the future? The present 
society in which we live, the church faces many, many complex 
problems from which the church cannot close its eyes, doing 
nothing. The situation now has become so acute, seemingly 
hopeless, that some people have come to lament, "what is a church 
doing.......?"" Some even question existence of God! 


In these times of social upheaval, the role of Japanese church in 
our society is becoming evermore important as younger generation of 
JA's becoming more and more conscious of their racial heritage. The 
very existence of Japanese churches in America give the younger 
generation of JA's sense of pride and help them to see clearly the 
mission that they must perform here in America--their country. The 
missions of Japanese churches are unique! We all must pray and 
strive for making our churches--"the house that is built on rock". 


As to us, Isseis, the days seem numbered on this earth, it is 
doubly important and meaningful that we do our best for Him. 
Because we do believe in Him. By truly believing, are we not 
promised of everlasting life?
