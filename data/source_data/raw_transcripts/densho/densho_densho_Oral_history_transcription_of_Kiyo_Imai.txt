was no violence, but some people quarrelled.and got injured. 
That kind of thing happened once in a while. I think people's 
temper become violent after staying in such a place for 3 years, 
Some people quarrelled and were injured, but it was not as bad as 
other camps. Those who quarrelled were mostly single people. 
There were hospital, dentists and optometrists, so wome people 


were very happy. 


Q: Do you remember Saburo Kido was beaten up at Poston? 
A: There were 3 camps in Poston, and we were in camp No. 3. Mr. Kido 
was in Camp No. 1. He had a terrible experience. He received a big 


injury. 


Q: Do you know the reason why he was beten up? 
A: I think he took American side. He did not say he was anti-Japanese, 


but it showed, so he was beaten up. 


Q: Weren't the pastors kind of watched? 
A: I did not hear such a thing, because the extremists were sent to 


a special camp, and some of them went back to Japan. All the 


radicals were put together in one camp. 


Q: Do you have any other recollection of the camp? 


: We were worried as the young people's discipline was loose. As 


my daughters had temptations, the life in camp was not at all 
happy. As far as the food was concerned, we had enough. We 

were used to poverty, so we had no complaint about food. ONly 
sometimes I wished I could cook some food, but that was out of 
wuestion. On cold nights, especially when it was raining, it was 
hard to go to the rest room. We had to go far to wash clothes, 
but we could not help it. 

As our second daughter had a very poor education in the 
camp for 3 years, it was hard for her when she came back to 
Dinuba and entered senior grade in the high school. When she 
enrolled in a junior colllege she had a difficult time. As she 
wanted to be a social worker, she took sociology and psychology. 
It would have been better if she took easier subjects. As it 
was so hard for her, she quit after junior college and did not 
go any higher, It is too bad she had to go to a school in camp. 


Had she stayed in Dinuba, she could have gone to an university. 


Q: Did you come back to Dinuba after the war was over? 


: Yes. We came back because we heard the Emperor said on the 


radio that Japan had surrendered. on August 15th. 


Q: How did you feel then? 


: I never felt sadder. I felt sorry for the Emperor. Inside our 


hearts we have feeling towards America as our children are 


Americans, but we also felt sorry for Japan as we are Japanese.

Imai is a gentle person, so he cried. In those days there was

Q: Looking back at the past, when was the hardest period? 

A: When I first went to Los Angeles I was lonesome, and 1 caught 
mumps. It was 1931, the first year they had the show, "The Life 
Of. .CHrist..” I went to see it on Hollywood Blivd. The next 
morning I came down with mumps. I had a hard time then. I was 
not used to American food yet. In the women's home where I was 
living, the food was not good, much less taste soy sauce. I was 
hurting and lonesome, but the people around me wand kind to me. 
That was my first bitter experience. 

I went to Los Angeles in July, and in October of the following 

year I moved to San Francisco. I had an unpleasant experience there. 
I lived in a women's home with many unfortunate women and children. 
There were 2 American officers; one was the supervisor of children, 
and the other was the manager. They had a nice bedroom and a rest 
room. My room was next to their rest room. The first thing one of 
the ladies said to me was, “Miss Izumi, please don't use our rest 
room." When I asked where I should go, I was told to use the child- 


ren's rest room which was very dirty. That was the most unplea- 


sant experience I had since I came to America, and I still cannot 


forget it. I do not have any other bad experience. As I told 
you before, I was pointed at at a store when we came back 
after the war, but that could not have been helped, and I was 
prepared for such a thing to happen. But when a missionary who 
was doing good work discriminated me, I had a bad impression. 


There was such a bad missionary. 


Q: Did these missionaries understand Japanese? 
A: No, they didn't. One was an old miss, and the other one was a 


widow. Both of them were good people. 


Q: Were the children Americans? 

A: There were a couple of mixed children, but most of them were 
children of divorced or separated Japanese parents. They lived 
there by cleaning and washing dishes, and they attended grammar 
schools. That was the only unpleasant experience I had in America. 
My room had a bunk bed like the children's, but the ladies had 
nice double beds. They cooked something special for their meals. 
That kind of a thing was not good for the children. 1 ate the 
same food with*the children. I still feel unpleasant when I 
think about that. I think Christians should not discriminate.

Q: When did you have the most financial difficulties? 

A: It was during the Depression. The Depression had started before 
we came to Dinuba in 1930. As I told you before, we received much 
presents while we were in Walnut Grove, so we had enough to live 
on. But when we came to Dinuba, people were in miserable condi- 
tion, and there was not even work for 10¢ an hour. Much less 
the ranchers were poor themselves, so there was no income for the 


church. That was the hardest time. 


Q: Did half of the pastor's salary come from the mission then? 

A: The financial aid from the Methodist Church also stopped as 
their church was in financial difficulties. That is why we used 
up all our er eh Mexicans in omr neighborhood had no shoes 
and looked very poor, we collected old clothes and sold them to 
the Mexicans. We did not have plenty, but God did not let us go 
hungry.not even for a day. We had faith in God. What would have

Q: Was Rev. Imai a big man? 
A: He was tall, around 5 feet 8 or 9. That is why our children are 


all tall. They don't like being tall, and say that they grew 


tall because I gave them too much milk. 


: As an Issei, is there anything you want to teach Sansei? 


: Since they have pride as Japanese, I think they should learn 


traditional Japanese gentleness. I think they should learn good 
points of Japanese characters. I think Sansei are better than 
Nisei. Nisei have both Japanese and American characters, so 

they are complicated, but Sanseis are completely Americans. 

I think they should learn Japanese language, even short sentences 
so that they can communicate with Issei. Some Sansei avoid Issei 


as they cannot speak Japanese. Otherwise, Sansei have good 


American character such as being frank.
