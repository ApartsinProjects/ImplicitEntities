When were you born? 

February 14, 1890. 

Where were you born? 

Hiroshima Prefecture. 

you remember about your parents? 

My mother died when I was 9, and I was brought up by a stepmother. 
That is why I came to America when I was 15 years old. 

How much education did you have in Japan? 

I came here right after I graduated from grammar school. 

What kind of a man was your father? 

My father did not want me to go to high school, so l decided 
to come to America. I was going to stay in Japan if I failed 
the physical examination but I passed, so I came here. 

Why didn't your father want to to go to high school? 

As I was the oldest son he was afraid I would not want to succeed 
the family trade if I went to high school. 

Did you have brothers or sisters? 

Yes, 2 brothers and 2 sisters. 

What do you remember about your childhood? 

In those days the compulsory education was 6 years, so they quit 
schol after 6 years, and only a few went to high schools. 

Was your father a farmer? 

Yes, he was growing rice and wheat. 

What was the religion of your family? 

Buddhism. 

Do you remember any war? 

I left Japan in 1905 after the Russo-Japanese War ended. I 
remember seeing Russian prisoners of war when I went to Yokohama. 

What part of 1905 did you leave Japan? 

I left Japan in September. 

What kind of people were on board the ship? 

There were many Japanese coming to America. The crew were mostly 
Chinese, but the captain was a white man. 

Were there any picture brides on board? 

No, there were no picture brides in those days. 

What did you do in Hawaii: 

I worked in a sugar plantation for 8 months. 

Were you a contract laborer? 

No, I wasn't. 

Did you go to Hawaii alone at the age of 15? 

A 16-year-old boy came with me.

Do you remember anything that happened on the ship? 

I was seasick, so there wasn't anything pleasant to remember. 

What did you do when you arrived in Hawaii? 

I went to a sugar plantation, and worked under a Chinese boss. 
At that. time I received only $18 a month. 

About how many people were working in your plantation? 

About 600 to 700 people. 

It was a big camp, wasn't it? 

No, it was not too big. Some camps had about 2,000 people. 

Were the laborers all Japanese? 

No, there were Chinese and Koreans, also, but the living 
quarters were separated. 

Tell me about the life in the camp. 

There were about 600 people in my camp at that time, and about 
10% of them were married. These married people built something 
like a cafeteria in the center of the camp and cooked meals. I 
did not eat in the cafeteria because some married couples from 
Hiroshima cooked for us. I did not know English, so I went to 
church every evening and learned English from Rev. Rokuro Abe. 

Was it a Japanese church? 

Yes, it was a Japanese church. Rev. Abe came to America later, 
and I met him here. 

Did those 600 people live in the same camp all the time, or did 
they move around? 

They stayed there ali the time because there was work in the 
Sugar cane field all the year round. Rev. Abe did interpre- 
ting and negotiating. I did not attend church on Sundays, 
but I went to church to learn English. 

Was the work in sugar cane field very hard? 

Yes, it was very hard because I was only 15 then. 

How many hours did you work everyday? 

Ten hours a day. 


: Did the foreman give you any consideration because you were young? 


No, but I did a man's job. 

Why did you leave Hawaii after 8 months? 

In those days we had $50 show money besides the passage. Most 
people sent it back to Japan after arriving in Hawaii, but I 
didn't as I wanted to come to America. That is why I had 
enough money to come to America. I arrived in San Francisco 
on June 20, 1906 right after the big earthquake, and there 
were no regular beds in hotels. 

Why did you decide to leave Hawaii: 

Hawaii is very small and noisy with many Japanese, so I didn't 
want to stay there. Anyway, I wanted to come to America, and 
Hawaii was a temporary place. I must have been on labor con- 
tract because I received a letter from my home telling me that 
they had to pay some fine for me as I broke the contract.

Q: Do you remember anything special that happened while you were 

in Jerome for 6 months? 

A: In other camps they burned coal, but in our camp there were 

many trees so we went out in the wood and cut down trees. 
We piled the logs on wagons pulled hy mule. It was hard 
to hitch the wagon on the mule without a harness. Luckily 
there were no wild mule. It rained all the time, and the 
water did not drain, so we had to go in the mud to catch 
the mule. 

Q: Did you get paid for cutting down tress? 

A: Yes, we did. The top pay was $19 a month, regular pay was $17, 
and women's pay was $13. Block managers like myself and chief 
cooks received $19. 

Q: Was there any other allowance? 

A: We received $4 a month as clothing allowance. 

Q: Was it given to everybody regardless of age? 

A: Yes, adults or children all received $4 a month. 

Q: Were there any other work? 

A: Yes, growing vegetables etc. 

Q: Was it in the camp? 

A: Yes, it was in the camp. There were farmers, firemen, policemen 
according to their profession. There were meetings of block 
managers once in a while. When people are inside the fence, 
their feelings are exposed, good or bad. 

Q: What was your keason for leaving Jerome in six months? 

A: My friends looked at me as if I was pro-American and no good 
for Japan, so it was not pjeasant.

; Yes, but Jerome was the first camp to be closed as it was close

Q: As we approach the end of this interview, as an Issei do you 
have any advice to young Sanseis and Yonseis? 

A: I may be prejudices, but I think there are very little suc- 
cessors of farmers. Young people seem to like going into 
easy-going white-collar jobs. Some white-collar people 
may make much money, but I think farming is not bad. It 
is not good all the time, but when it is good we have much 
more income than the white-collar people. I regret that they 
do not like farming. I think the farmers lead carefree lives 
without being bound by time. For example, in most places in 
America fynerals are held at night. Only in Fresno 80% of 
funerals are held around 2 o'clock in the afternoon. This is 
because farmers have free time. No matter how rich you are, 
if you are bound by time and have no freedom it is no good. 

I think we should think about this. In 5 or 10 years when 
all the Isseis are gone, Sanseis and Yonseis would not be 
able to have funerals in the afternoon. I don't want them 

to become farmers just because of funerals. I think if we 
have time we can contribute to the society. From that reason 
I encourage people to become farmers. 

Q: Do you have any instruction to young people.

Will you tell me about the Kikaken Kisei Domei? 

When we came back from the camps, the Land Law was very strict. 
Some interested person met in Stockton and decided to do some- 
thing about the Land Law. It was written in the Land Law that 
aliens who could not be naturalized could not own land. So we 
had to attain naturalization privilege, and at the same oppose 
the Land Law.Therefore we decided to send Mike Masaoka of JACL 
to Washington, and on the other hand we would raise $250,000 
aS the campaign fund. That is how the Kikaken Kisei Domei was 
organized in February 1946. 

Then it was right after you came back from the camp, wasm't it? 
Yes, because people were arrested here and there. At that time 
the government proposed a compromising plan that if we paid 
half of the value of the land, they will recognize the owner- 
Ship of the land. There were more than ten people at that time 
who paid half the price of the land and had the ownership of 
their land recognized. We did not know how it would be in the 

future. There were more landowners in Fresno area in Central 

California than any other ‘place, so they raise $250,000 by 
collecting $2 an acre from lnadowers and $1 an acre from 
those who farmed by contract. Then we Set up a headquarter 
in San Francisco with Mr. Sugiyama as the secretary, and 
Sent Mike Masaoka to Washington. Since our campaign started 
I went to San Francisco twice or three times a year for con- 
ference. It was very hard to pass the Senate. Finally in the 
7th year we asked Senator Walter of Minnesota to present our 
proposal on the naturalization, and asked the Secretary of 
State Dean Achson to act as our attorney. When Mr. Achson 
testified that anybody who was born in this country has the 
citizenship, the Land Act was nullified. ; 

Do many people know about the Kikaken Kisei Domei? 

Only ones who worked for it were Mr. Kanagawa, Mr. Sakamoto 
and myself. 

About how many people benefited by Kikaken Kisei Domei? 

All the Isseis did. But people in Southern California did not 
contribute any because most landowners were in Central Cali- 
fornia. 

I heard that some Japanese farmers around Fresno organized a 
farmers' unién and fought against the depression. 

It must be the Sun Maid Raisin Association. It was organized 
around 1907. They must have joined it. 

Were the members of this union all Japanese? 

No, they were mostly white people. Japanese were not powerful 
to organized a union then.
