And do you know about their life in Japan, and their parents’ lives in Japan? 

No. What I know about my father is that he was a younger son, probably around number 
six or number seven. And the two older brothers fulfilled the military obligations of the 
family. They were a samurai family, and the firstborn was expected to become an officer 
in the navy, and the second born was supposed to become an officer in the army. And 
then, subsequent boys were allowed to choose either the army or the navy. My father — he 
had graduated high school at age 18 — decided that he did not particularly care for the 
militaristic viewpoint of his government, and so he decided to immigrate to the United 
States. And, for that reason, he left. 

Did any of his siblings or other family members immigrate to the United States? 

No, he was the only one that did, and the rest of his siblings remained in Japan. And I did 
not know the names of any one of them, nor did I ever correspond with any of them. My 
father did correspond once in a while, never talked with them on the telephone, and never 
returned to Japan. 

Do you know what his family felt about him leaving? Did he ever talk about that? 

No, he did not. I guess they accepted the fact that, since the boys had fulfilled their 
military obligations, they had no ties to him. So, they just accepted his decision.

their rates for college entrance. So, the schooling was good in the area that my father and

all of the produce and inventory that he had in the store, and then we had to move back to 
Skid Row, LA. And, instead of going bankrupt, what he did was, he paid off all his bills 
later on, after he was able to find work, after leaving his owner-operated business 
himself.

Yeah. My best friend was Donald McKenzie [phonetic]. And he lived probably about

So, this is from the west side. I had no real friends when we lived in Skid Row. I didn't 
have many contacts with young people at that time, because the area itself was unsavory, 
and I wasn't in the elementary school long enough. I started elementary school when I 
was four. My mother thought I was ready to go early, and so, I went to Amelia Street 
School. It's a public school in Downtown LA, where I was, at age four, an interpreter- 
translator for the elementary school. Miss Adams, a teacher, Miss Adams, was perplexed 
by the influx of Japanese American students who could not speak English. Their families 
did not react the way my parents did. My parents wanted us to be American, and so, they 
spoke as much English as they could, mixed in with Japanese. But then, with English, so 
that when I went to elementary school, I was bilingual to such an extent that I was able to 
help the elementary school teacher give instructions to other children, and then, also, 
interpret their remarks back to her. So, I was held back in kindergarten, and so I must 
have been in kindergarten for two years instead of one.

Japanese school for one hour after regular school, and then four hours on Saturday. And I 
thought that was a real infringement on my right as a student, and so, after finishing about 
three years of Japanese school, I quit. And then, when I was in about the sixth grade or 
so, my father insisted that I go back to Japanese school, because he wanted me to learn 
Japanese. And when I was able to finish a college education, he would be able to get me a 
job, because he had connections in Japan with a former classmate. He would be able to 
get me a job in Japan, and my future, then, would be assured, insofar as he was 
concerned. And so, I reluctantly agreed that I would go back. And my first year of work 
was done very quickly, because I had already done three years. So, the first year went 
real fast. The second year took me a little longer. The third year was harder, and it was 
very difficult, because the fellow students were probably a foot shorter than I, since they 
were much younger. And they were doing much better than I in Japanese, and I got 
enough of it. And so, I quit again at the end of the third year of Japanese. So, I've quit 
twice in that third grade, and this time my parents stopped insisting. They decided that I 
must be able to figure out the need for Japanese language, and so they gave up on me. So, 
I know that little in Japanese, which means that I'm illiterate. I really can speak a little. I 
cannot read or write Japanese, except, of course, the very simple — I've kind of found out 
hiragana, but then, when it came to the ideographs, I'm completely lost.* So, my use of 


Japanese is, at best, less than minimal.
