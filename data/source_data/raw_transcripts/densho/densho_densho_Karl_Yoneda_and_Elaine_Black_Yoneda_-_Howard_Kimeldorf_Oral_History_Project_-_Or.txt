[00:06:56] HOWARD: What were you saying in 1933 when you got onto a soapbox on the waterfront? 


[00:07:00] ELAINE: It was anti-war; fascism was just beginning to rise, and we really didn’t realize how

arresting and breaking up meetings. Street meetings were being broken up by the Red Squad, and the charges 
were refusing to move, disturbing the peace, distributing leaflets. 


[00:07:40] HOWARD: Did you ever talk about concrete grievances that longshoremen might have experienced

[00:07:46] ELAINE: No, I don’t recall that. As far as what happened within the union structure, that really 
wasn’t my role to say, “Why do you want $1/hour? Why don’t you want more?” That was not my role. My role 
was, if there’d been any arrests, to issue leaflets around the cases asking people to pack the courtrooms. So 
these people who were arrested, whose rights were violated—the right to gather and protest and to meet and to 
speak were being violated. That was the role, to point out the violation of the Bill of Rights and other rights that 
we thought we had and which were being violated by the police who were instruments of the industrial 
association and the ship owners.
