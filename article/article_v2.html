IRC-Bench: A Benchmark for Implicit Entity Recognition
in Oral History Narratives

Eden Moran, Yehudit Aperstein, Sasha Aperstein
School of Software Engineering: Intelligent Systems

Abstract

Personal narratives in oral histories are replete with implicit references to people, places, and events that speakers assume their audience can identify from context alone. We introduce Implicit Entity Recognition (IER), the task of identifying entities that are contextually referenced but never explicitly named in text, and release IRC-Bench, a benchmark of 25,136 samples derived from 12,337 unique entities across 1,994 oral history transcripts spanning 13 thematic domains. Each sample consists of an Entity-Grounded Narrative (EGN) containing the entity name and an Entity-Elided Narrative (EEN) from which all explicit mentions have been removed; systems must recover the correct entity given only the EEN. We identify a key structural property of implicit references, their non-locality: cues are distributed across multiple clauses rather than concentrated in a single mention span, fundamentally distinguishing IER from named entity recognition and entity linking. We evaluate open-world LLM inference, closed-world retrieval, and hybrid approaches across multiple model scales. GPT-4o few-shot achieves 31.6% exact match (41.1% alias-aware Hit@1) in the open-world setting, while fine-tuned DPR reaches 30.0% Hit@1 (63.3% Hit@10) in the closed-world setting. Chain-of-thought prompting consistently degrades performance relative to direct few-shot inference, and retrieval-augmented generation underperforms direct LLM prediction. All data, code, and evaluation tools are publicly released.

Keywords: implicit entity recognition, oral history, non-locality, large language models, dense passage retrieval, benchmark construction, entity linking, Wikidata

Introduction

Consider the following passage from a Japanese American oral history interview:

Entity-Grounded Narrative (EGN)

"The attack on Pearl Harbor was the event that changed everything for Japanese Americans like me. After December 7, 1941, suspicion and hatred grew, and we were treated as enemy aliens despite being American citizens. It was because of Pearl Harbor that the government issued Executive Order 9066 and started the forced relocation."

Entity-Elided Narrative (EEN)

"The surprise attack on a naval base in Hawaii was the event that changed everything for Japanese Americans like me. After December 7, 1941, suspicion and hatred grew, and we were treated as enemy aliens despite being American citizens. It was because of that attack that the government issued an order and started the forced relocation."

Gold entity: Attack on Pearl Harbor (Q52418)  |  Type: Event  |  Cues: December 7, 1941; naval base in Hawaii; Executive Order 9066; forced relocation

A human reader readily identifies the Attack on Pearl Harbor from the constellation of cues: the date, the Hawaiian naval base, the executive order, the internment of Japanese Americans. No single phrase names the entity; instead, recognition depends on integrating cultural, temporal, and historical knowledge distributed across the entire passage. This pattern of implicit entity reference is pervasive in oral histories, where speakers routinely allude to well-known people, places, and events without naming them, relying on shared background knowledge with their interviewer.

Existing NLP tasks do not address this phenomenon. Named Entity Recognition (NER) identifies explicitly mentioned entity spans in text [1, 2]. Entity Linking (EL) resolves those spans to knowledge base entries [3, 4]. Coreference resolution connects multiple references to the same entity, but requires at least one explicit mention as an antecedent [5]. In implicit entity references, the entity is never named anywhere in the text; there is no span to extract, no mention to link, no antecedent to resolve. The task is fundamentally generative: the system must synthesize an entity identity from distributed contextual cues.

We introduce Implicit Entity Recognition (IER) as a distinct NLP task and release IRC-Bench (Implicit Reminiscence Context Benchmark), a large-scale evaluation resource constructed from real oral history transcripts. IER matters for several practical reasons. Oral history archives containing millions of hours of recorded testimony remain largely inaccessible to structured search because the entities discussed are rarely stated by name [6]. Conversational AI systems that interact with elderly users for reminiscence therapy must identify the people and events being discussed even when the speaker does not name them [7, 8]. Information retrieval over personal narratives requires understanding not just what is said, but what is meant.

Our contributions are as follows:

  1. Task definition. We formalize Implicit Entity Recognition as a novel NLP task, distinguishing it from NER, entity linking, and coreference resolution. We define the non-locality property of implicit references: entity cues are distributed across multiple non-contiguous text spans, requiring holistic integration rather than local pattern matching.
  2. OralHistoryCorpus (OHC). We assemble a cleaned corpus of 1,994 oral history transcripts from 25 public collections spanning 13 thematic domains (veterans, September 11, civil rights, immigration, COVID-19, Depression era, Japanese American internment, labor, academic, regional, refugees, disasters, and art).
  3. IRC-Bench. We release a benchmark of 25,136 IER samples derived from 12,337 unique Wikidata-linked entities, with entity-level train/dev/test splits ensuring zero entity overlap between partitions. Each sample includes both an EGN and an EEN, along with entity metadata (QID, aliases, Wikipedia description).
  4. Empirical evaluation. We systematically compare open-world LLM inference, closed-world dense retrieval, and hybrid approaches across multiple model scales and prompting strategies, revealing that chain-of-thought reasoning degrades IER performance and that model scale is the dominant factor in open-world accuracy.

Related Work

Named Entity Recognition

Named Entity Recognition identifies and classifies explicit entity mentions in text. Classical approaches relied on handcrafted features and conditional random fields [1], while modern systems employ deep learning architectures including BiLSTM-CRF [9], transformer-based sequence labeling [10], and large language model prompting [11, 12]. Recent benchmarks such as CoNLL-2003 [13] and MultiCoNER [14] have driven progress across entity types and languages. The W2NER framework [15] unified flat, nested, and discontinuous NER as word-word relation classification, and UniversalNER [16] demonstrated targeted distillation from LLMs for open-domain entity extraction. Despite these advances, all NER formulations assume the target entity appears as an explicit surface form in the input text, an assumption that does not hold for implicit references.

Entity Linking

Entity Linking resolves textual mentions to entries in a knowledge base. Neural approaches include local attention models [3], bi-encoder architectures such as BLINK [17], autoregressive generation via GENRE [18], and efficient zero-shot systems like ReFinED [19]. Botha et al. [20] extended entity linking to over 100 languages. These systems take an identified mention span as input and rank candidate entities; they cannot operate when no mention span exists. IER requires generating entity candidates from distributed contextual cues rather than resolving a given span.

Implicit and Zero-Mention Entities

Limited prior work has addressed entities that are referenced but not named. Hosseini [21] introduced implicit entity recognition in tweets, and Hosseini and Bagheri [22] developed learning-to-rank methods for implicit entities on Twitter. Perera et al. [23] explored implicit entity recognition in clinical documents. However, these efforts target short, formulaic text (tweets) or highly structured domains (clinical notes). Oral history narratives present a fundamentally different challenge: passages are longer, cues are more diffuse, and entity identification requires integrating cultural and historical knowledge rather than exploiting domain-specific templates. No prior benchmark exists for implicit entity recognition in narrative text.

Oral History NLP

Computational analysis of oral histories has received growing attention. Technology-assisted reminiscence systems have been developed for dementia care [7, 8], and AI-driven conversational agents have been explored as companions for elderly users [24, 25]. Digital storytelling platforms combining AI with augmented reality enable communities to preserve personal narratives [26]. However, these systems primarily facilitate memory recall and do not attempt to recover the implicit entities that speakers reference without naming.

Knowledge-Grounded Question Answering

The closest existing task to IER is knowledge-grounded question answering, where a system must reason over both a text passage and an external knowledge base to produce an answer [27, 28]. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) approaches retrieve relevant knowledge base passages and condition generation on them [29, 30]. While IER shares the requirement for external knowledge, it differs in that the "question" is an entire narrative rather than a targeted query, and the answer is always a single entity rather than a free-form text span. Furthermore, IER exhibits the non-locality property: the relevant cues are distributed throughout the passage rather than concentrated near a question token. This structural difference, as we show empirically, causes standard RAG pipelines to underperform direct LLM inference.

Task Definition

Problem Formulation

Let \(\mathcal{K}\) denote a knowledge base of entities, where each entity \(e \in \mathcal{K}\) is associated with a canonical name, a set of aliases \(A(e)\), an entity type \(\tau(e) \in \{\texttt{Person}, \texttt{Place}, \texttt{Organization}, \texttt{Event}, \texttt{Work}, \texttt{Military\_Unit}\}\), and a textual description \(d(e)\). Given an input text \(T\) in which some entity \(e^*\) is implicitly referenced but never explicitly named, the task of Implicit Entity Recognition (IER) is to predict \(e^*\).

We distinguish two evaluation settings:

Open-world IER. The system must produce the entity name directly, with no access to a candidate list. Formally, the system implements a function \(f_{\text{open}}: T \rightarrow \hat{e}\) that maps input text to a predicted entity string. Evaluation compares \(\hat{e}\) against the gold entity \(e^*\) and its aliases \(A(e^*)\) using exact match, containment, and alias-aware metrics.

Closed-world IER. The system is given access to a candidate set \(\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{K}\) and must rank candidates by relevance to the input text. The system implements a scoring function \(s: (T, e) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) for all \(e \in \mathcal{C}\), and evaluation uses Hit@K and nDCG@K over the ranked list.

Distinction from Related Tasks

IER is fundamentally distinct from three related tasks. In Named Entity Recognition, the entity \(e^*\) appears as an explicit surface form \(m \subset T\); the system extracts \(m\). In Entity Linking, the system is given the mention span \(m\) and resolves it to \(e^* \in \mathcal{K}\). In Coreference Resolution, multiple references (including pronouns) are clustered, but at least one explicit mention serves as an antecedent. In IER, no surface form of \(e^*\) exists in \(T\); the entity must be inferred entirely from contextual cues.

The Non-Locality Property

We define a key structural property that distinguishes IER from span-based entity tasks. Let \(C(T, e^*) = \{c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_n\}\) denote the set of textual cues in \(T\) that collectively identify \(e^*\). In standard NER and EL, the entity is localized: there exists a contiguous span \(m\) that is sufficient to identify \(e^*\). In IER, the entity is non-local:

$$\nexists \; m \subset T \;\text{s.t.}\; m \;\text{is contiguous and}\; m \Rightarrow e^* $$ $$\text{but} \quad C(T, e^*) \Rightarrow e^*, \quad \text{where cues } c_i \text{ are non-contiguous in } T$$

That is, no single contiguous substring of \(T\) is sufficient to identify \(e^*\), but the set of distributed cues collectively determines it. This non-locality has direct implications for method design: approaches that rely on local span matching (NER, EL) or single-vector passage encoding (dense retrieval) are structurally disadvantaged relative to approaches that can integrate information across the full text (LLMs with sufficient context windows).

Acquisition Pipeline

Transcripts were acquired through a multi-stage pipeline. For each collection, we identified the public archive URL and downloaded raw transcript files in their native format (HTML, PDF, XML, or plain text). Collections hosted by the Library of Congress, the National Park Service, and university digital libraries were accessed through their respective APIs or web interfaces. The Federal Writers' Project materials were sourced from the Library of Congress American Memory collection. Densho transcripts were obtained from the Densho Digital Repository. COVID-19 oral histories were drawn from the Smithsonian National Museum of American History and related public projects.

Cleaning Pipeline

Raw transcripts underwent a three-stage cleaning process. First, format normalization converted all source formats to plain text, removing HTML markup, PDF artifacts, page headers/footers, and interviewer annotations. Second, structural parsing segmented each transcript into speaker turns, identifying interviewer and narrator roles. Third, an LLM-based cleaning pass using GPT-4.1-mini corrected OCR errors, normalized inconsistent spellings, resolved encoding artifacts, and standardized formatting. Each cleaned transcript was serialized to a structured YAML format containing metadata fields (title, narrator, interviewer, date, collection, source URL) and the segmented transcript body.

Quality Assurance

Automated quality checks verified that each transcript contained valid metadata, at least 200 words of narrator speech, and no residual markup or encoding errors. A stratified random sample of 100 transcripts (4 per collection) was manually reviewed for fidelity to the original source material, correct speaker attribution, and absence of introduced artifacts. The rejection rate at manual review was below 3%, with most issues stemming from ambiguous speaker attribution in multi-party interviews.

IRC-Bench Construction

Overview

IRC-Bench is constructed through a five-stage pipeline that transforms raw oral history transcripts into validated IER evaluation samples. Figure 1 illustrates the full pipeline. Each stage is described in detail below, followed by dataset statistics and quality validation procedures.

Figure 1: IRC-Bench construction pipeline. Raw oral history transcripts undergo named entity recognition, Wikidata linking, entity-grounded narrative generation, entity elision, and automated validation to produce IER evaluation samples.

Named Entity Extraction

The first stage extracts named entities from cleaned transcripts using GPT-4.1-mini with structured output prompts. For each transcript, the model identifies entities of six types (Person, Place, Organization, Event, Work, Military_Unit) along with the surrounding context in which each entity is mentioned. Entities that appear only in passing (single-word references with no surrounding descriptive context) are discarded, as they cannot support meaningful implicit reference construction.

Wikidata Entity Linking

Each extracted entity is linked to its Wikidata entry via a multi-step resolution process. First, exact string matching against Wikidata labels identifies high-confidence matches. For entities without exact matches, fuzzy matching and LLM-assisted disambiguation are applied. For each linked entity, we retrieve the Wikidata QID, the canonical name, all registered aliases, the short description, and (when available) the opening paragraph of the corresponding Wikipedia article. Entities that cannot be linked to Wikidata with high confidence are excluded. The final knowledge base contains 12,337 unique entities, each with a validated QID.

Entity-Grounded Narrative Generation

For each (entity, transcript) pair, GPT-4.1-mini generates an Entity-Grounded Narrative (EGN): a coherent paragraph that summarizes the transcript context surrounding the entity mention while retaining the entity name. The EGN is written in the first person to preserve the oral history register and is constrained to 50 to 150 words. The prompt instructs the model to include contextual details (dates, locations, associated people, sensory descriptions) that could serve as identification cues for the entity.

Entity Elision

The Entity-Elided Narrative (EEN) is produced by a second LLM pass that rewrites the EGN with all explicit mentions of the target entity removed. The model replaces the entity name and any unambiguous references (e.g., acronyms, well-known nicknames) with generic descriptions while preserving all contextual cues. For instance, "Pearl Harbor" becomes "a naval base in Hawaii," and "Executive Order 9066" may be reduced to "an order." The EEN must remain natural and grammatically correct; it should read as a plausible oral history passage rather than a text with blanks.

Validation

Each generated sample undergoes three automated validation checks. Leak detection verifies that neither the entity name nor any of its Wikidata aliases appear in the EEN, using both exact string matching and fuzzy matching with a similarity threshold. Naturalness scoring uses a language model to rate whether the EEN reads as fluent, natural text. Recoverability assessment verifies that a strong LLM (GPT-4o) can recover the gold entity from the EEN at a rate significantly above random, confirming that sufficient cues are preserved. Samples failing any check are regenerated (up to three attempts) or discarded.

Dataset Splits

IRC-Bench uses entity-level splitting to prevent information leakage. The 12,337 unique entities are partitioned into training (70%), development (10%), and test (20%) sets with zero entity overlap between partitions. All samples associated with a given entity are assigned to the same partition. This ensures that models cannot memorize entity-specific cue patterns during training and then exploit them at test time. Table 2 reports the partition statistics.

Experimental Setup

Evaluation Settings

We evaluate IER under three paradigms that span the spectrum from unconstrained generation to constrained ranking. All experiments use the IRC-Bench test set (4,633 samples, 2,468 unique entities) with zero entity overlap against training data.

Open-world methods (O1 through O12) receive only the Entity-Elided Narrative and must produce the entity name directly, with no access to a candidate list. We test four LLM families: GPT-4o, GPT-4.1-mini, Llama 3.1 8B Instruct, and Llama 3.2 3B Instruct. For each model, we evaluate zero-shot (ZS), few-shot (FS, 3 exemplars drawn from the training set), and chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting strategies where applicable. Few-shot exemplars are selected to cover diverse entity types and are held constant across all test samples.

Closed-world methods (C1 through C6) rank the full entity knowledge base (12,337 entities) by relevance to the input narrative. We compare two dense retrieval architectures: BGE-base-en-v1.5 [31] used off-the-shelf, and DPR [30] fine-tuned on IRC-Bench training pairs. For each retriever, we encode entities using three alternative representations: the entity name alone, the Wikidata short description, and the opening paragraph of the corresponding Wikipedia article. The query is always the raw EEN text.

Hybrid methods (RAG1) combine retrieval and generation. RAG1 uses BGE retrieval to obtain the top-20 candidate entities, reranks them with a cross-encoder (MiniLM-L6), and presents the top-5 candidates as context to GPT-4.1-mini, which then selects or generates the final entity prediction.

Evaluation Metrics

Open-world methods are evaluated by exact string match between the predicted entity name and the gold canonical name. We additionally report alias-aware Hit@1, a four-tier matching scheme: (1) exact match with the canonical name, (2) containment (the prediction contains or is contained by the gold name), (3) match against any Wikidata alias, and (4) alias containment. Closed-world methods are evaluated by Hit@1, Hit@10, and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) over the ranked candidate list. Statistical significance is assessed using McNemar's test for paired binary outcomes (correct/incorrect), with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons.

Implementation Details

GPT-4o and GPT-4.1-mini experiments use the OpenAI API with temperature 0 and a maximum output length of 128 tokens. Llama models are served locally on a single NVIDIA A10 GPU using vLLM with 4-bit quantization. DPR fine-tuning uses the standard bi-encoder architecture with hard negatives mined from BGE retrieval; we train for 20 epochs with a learning rate of 2e-5 and batch size 32. All code, prompts, and configuration files are released with the benchmark.

Results

Open-World Performance

Table 4 presents the open-world results. GPT-4o with few-shot prompting (O2) achieves the highest exact match accuracy at 31.6%, rising to 41.1% under alias-aware evaluation. Model scale is the dominant factor: moving from Llama 3.2 1B (1.3%) to Llama 3.1 8B (17.8%) to GPT-4o (31.6%) yields roughly log-linear gains. Few-shot prompting consistently improves over zero-shot across all model sizes (p < 0.001 by McNemar's test), with absolute gains ranging from 1.5 percentage points (Llama 1B) to 4.6 points (GPT-4o).

The most striking finding is the failure of chain-of-thought prompting. CoT reduces GPT-4.1-mini accuracy from 28.7% (few-shot) to 19.0%, and GPT-4o accuracy from 31.6% to 19.6%. This represents a 34% relative degradation for GPT-4.1-mini and a 38% relative degradation for GPT-4o. We analyze the reasons for this failure in Section 8.

The hybrid RAG approach (19.7%) underperforms even GPT-4.1-mini zero-shot (25.1%). Adding retrieved candidates to the prompt appears to introduce noise that overwhelms the LLM's ability to integrate the distributed cues in the narrative. Direct generation, where the model attends holistically to the full input passage, proves more effective than retrieval-augmented prediction.

Closed-World Performance

Table 5 shows the closed-world retrieval results. Fine-tuned DPR with description representations (C5) achieves the best performance: 35.4% Hit@1, 71.5% Hit@10, and 0.475 MRR. The comparison between off-the-shelf BGE and fine-tuned DPR reveals the magnitude of domain adaptation benefits. DPR fine-tuning nearly doubles Hit@1 (16.5% to 30.0% for name representations) and increases Hit@10 by 26.5 absolute points.

Across both retrieval architectures, entity description representations consistently outperform name-only and Wikipedia representations. This pattern is intuitive: descriptions provide a concise, attribute-rich summary that aligns well with the contextual cues present in elided narratives. Full Wikipedia articles, despite containing more information, introduce noise from tangential content. Name-only representations lack the semantic richness needed to match distributed contextual cues.

Error Analysis

Table 6 reports the error distribution across 200 sampled errors per model. The dominant error category is same-type unrelated (42 to 52%), where the model predicts an entity of the correct type but with no meaningful connection to the gold entity. For example, when the gold entity is a specific military base in Virginia, the model might predict a different military base in Texas. Wrong-type errors account for 22 to 35% of failures, often reflecting confusion between Organizations and Places (e.g., predicting a city when the gold entity is a university in that city). Same-type related errors (13 to 25%) represent near-misses where the predicted entity is semantically close to the gold, such as predicting a parent organization instead of a subsidiary. Hallucination is rare (<2.5%), indicating that models almost always predict entities that exist, even when they predict the wrong one.

Entity Type Difficulty

Person entities are the most challenging across all methods, with accuracy roughly 40% below the dataset average. Persons in oral histories are often described through idiosyncratic personal anecdotes (e.g., "my commanding officer who always wore a red scarf") rather than through publicly documented attributes, making them difficult for models that rely on parametric world knowledge. Event and Military_Unit entities are the easiest, as they tend to be described through distinctive combinations of dates, locations, and named participants that narrow the candidate space.

Analysis and Discussion

Why Chain-of-Thought Hurts

The consistent degradation under CoT prompting is perhaps the most counterintuitive finding of this study. In tasks such as mathematical reasoning and multi-hop question answering, CoT improves performance by decomposing complex reasoning into intermediate steps [32]. IER, however, does not reward step-by-step decomposition. Identifying an implicit entity requires holistic pattern matching: the model must simultaneously attend to a constellation of distributed cues (dates, locations, associated people, sensory details) and match this constellation against its parametric knowledge of entities. When forced to reason step by step, the model tends to fixate on individual cues in isolation, often arriving at a locally plausible but globally incorrect entity. For instance, a CoT trace might correctly identify "December 7, 1941" and "Hawaii" but then erroneously conclude the entity is "Hickam Air Force Base" rather than "Attack on Pearl Harbor," because the step-by-step process loses sight of the broader narrative context. This finding suggests that IER belongs to a class of tasks where recognition is fundamentally gestalt: the whole (the cue constellation) is more informative than the sum of its parts (individual cues analyzed sequentially).

Why RAG Underperforms

The RAG pipeline's poor showing (19.7% vs. 28.7% for direct few-shot) reflects a mismatch between retrieval and the non-locality property of implicit references. Dense retrievers encode the query (the EEN) as a single vector, which must capture the entire constellation of distributed cues. In practice, the single-vector bottleneck loses fine-grained cue information, and the retrieved candidates are often topically related but incorrect. When these noisy candidates are presented to the LLM as context, they can override the model's own correct intuition. We observe cases where the LLM without RAG correctly identifies the entity, but with RAG selects a plausible-looking but wrong candidate from the retrieved list. The retriever thus acts as a bottleneck rather than an aid, filtering out the correct entity before the LLM ever sees it.

Non-Locality and Global Attention

The non-locality property defined in Section 3 has direct empirical consequences. Methods that can attend to the full input passage (LLMs with sufficient context windows) consistently outperform methods that compress the input to a fixed-size representation (dense retrievers). Within the LLM paradigm, larger models with greater capacity for cross-passage attention outperform smaller ones. This pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that IER fundamentally requires global attention over distributed cues, and that local or compressed representations are structurally insufficient for the task.

The Open-Closed Gap

Comparing the best open-world result (O2: 31.6% exact, 41.1% alias-aware) with the best closed-world result (C5: 35.4% Hit@1) reveals a modest advantage for the closed-world setting. This is notable because the closed-world system has access to the full entity knowledge base (12,337 candidates), which one might expect to provide a substantial advantage. The relatively small gap suggests that the primary bottleneck in IER is not entity recall (knowing that the entity exists) but entity recognition (matching the cue constellation to the correct entity). LLMs possess broad parametric knowledge that partially compensates for the lack of an explicit candidate list, while the retriever's single-vector encoding partially negates the advantage of having the full candidate set. The closed-world setting does, however, offer a much stronger Hit@10 (71.5%), indicating that the correct entity is often in the retriever's shortlist even when it is not ranked first. This suggests that combining fine-tuned retrieval with LLM reranking is a promising direction for future work.

What Makes Entities Hard

Entity difficulty correlates with two factors: the specificity of available cues and the density of the entity's "neighborhood" in the knowledge base. Events and Military Units tend to have highly specific cue combinations (unique dates, named participants, particular locations) that dramatically narrow the candidate space. Persons, by contrast, are often described through generic biographical attributes (occupation, approximate era, region) shared by many candidates, making disambiguation difficult. Organizations fall between these extremes: well-known organizations (e.g., the United Nations) are easy, while local organizations (e.g., a specific veterans' club) are nearly impossible without very specific contextual cues.

Conclusion

We have introduced Implicit Entity Recognition as a novel NLP task and released IRC-Bench, a benchmark of 25,136 samples spanning 12,337 Wikidata-linked entities from 1,994 oral history transcripts. Our systematic evaluation across open-world, closed-world, and hybrid paradigms reveals several key findings. First, model scale is the dominant factor in open-world accuracy, with performance spanning from 1.3% (Llama 1B) to 31.6% (GPT-4o). Second, chain-of-thought prompting consistently degrades performance, suggesting that IER requires holistic pattern matching rather than sequential reasoning. Third, retrieval-augmented generation underperforms direct LLM inference due to the non-locality of implicit cues. Fourth, domain-specific fine-tuning of dense retrievers nearly doubles retrieval Hit@1, and entity description representations consistently outperform name-only and full-article representations.

Several limitations should be noted. The benchmark is constructed from English-language oral histories focused primarily on American experiences, limiting cross-lingual and cross-cultural generalization. The entity elision process, though validated, is LLM-generated and may not perfectly replicate the patterns of naturally occurring implicit references. The alias-aware evaluation, while more permissive than exact match, still penalizes predictions that are semantically correct but use an unregistered surface form.

Future work should explore several promising directions: multi-modal IER incorporating audio features from the original recordings, cross-lingual benchmarks constructed from oral history archives in other languages, active learning approaches that combine retriever shortlists with LLM reranking, and the development of specialized architectures that explicitly model the non-locality property of implicit entity cues through structured attention over distributed text spans.
