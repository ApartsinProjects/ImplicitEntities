Abstract

Loneliness among the elderly is a growing public health concern, and reminiscence therapy offers a promising intervention by reconnecting individuals through shared life experiences. However, personal narratives are often fragmented and unstructured, with key contextual elements (people, places, events) referenced implicitly rather than by name. We introduce the task of Implicit Reminiscence Context (IRC) recovery, formalized as a ranking problem over latent entity mentions, and release IRC-Bench, a curated benchmark of 3,347 samples across four variants (two real, two synthetic), two domains (veterans' recollections and tweets), eight entity types, and 814 unique entities, with LLM-audited gold labels, multi-reference annotations, Wikidata entity links, and entity-level open-set evaluation splits. We identify a key theoretical property of implicit references: their non-locality, where entity descriptions are distributed across multiple non-contiguous cues rather than occupying a single mention span, distinguishing IRC from standard entity linking. We systematically compare five recognition paradigms (direct LLM inference, closed-set constrained generation, embedding-based retrieval, hybrid RAG, and DPR fine-tuned retrieval), four LLM backbones (Gemini 2.0 Flash, Llama 3.1 8B, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-3.5-turbo), multiple embedding models (MiniLM-L6-v2, MPNet-base-v2, BGE-base-en-v1.5, E5-base-v2), and prompt strategies (zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought). On the curated benchmark, closed-set recognition achieves Hit@1 = 0.491 on veterans' narratives and 0.728 on tweets, while open-set LLM inference reaches 0.390 and 0.728 respectively. A comprehensive gold-label audit reveals that 89% of apparent model failures stem from annotation errors rather than model limitations. Evaluation employs a five-tier matching hierarchy (exact, containment, Jaccard, WordNet synonym, LLM alias) alongside Precision@K, Recall@K, nDCG@K, and semantic similarity metrics with Wikidata entity linking for knowledge-grounded assessment.

Keywords: implicit entity recognition, reminiscence therapy, large language models, retrieval-augmented generation, benchmark construction, entity linking, semantic evaluation

Datasets: The IRC-Bench Benchmark

A key contribution of this work is IRC-Bench, a rigorously curated benchmark for evaluating implicit entity recognition across domains and generation strategies. The benchmark was constructed through a thirteen-step pipeline encompassing source data collection, text transformation, synthetic generation, multi-stage quality control, gold label auditing, and knowledge-base linking. The full benchmark, including data, splits, entity descriptions, evaluation code, and Wikidata entity links, is publicly released. A detailed exploratory data analysis is provided in Appendix H.

Source Data Collection

IRC-Bench draws from two complementary source domains. The veterans domain uses interview transcripts from the U.S. Library of Congress Veterans History Project (loc.gov/vets), a publicly accessible archive of oral histories from American military veterans. Ten narrators' interviews were selected and segmented into short units, each capturing a single detail or event. The Twitter domain incorporates the annotated dataset of implicit entity tweets from Hosseini [30, 31], originally collected at Toronto Metropolitan University, spanning 273 entities across 59 fine-grained types.

Text-Based Transformation (T2E)

The T2E pipeline transforms real texts containing explicit entity mentions into implicit versions. For veterans' transcripts, explicit entity mentions were replaced with implicit descriptions using type-aware rewrite rules: persons were replaced with roles or achievements; places with geographic or cultural descriptors; events with timeframes, actions, or outcomes. Entities were extracted via an LLM-based extractor and a modern NER model, canonicalized using an alias map, and validated for name-leak absence. The Twitter T2E data (851 annotated tweets from Hosseini [30, 31]) was incorporated directly with its original annotations. T2E data preserves the natural language patterns, cultural specificity, and narrative complexity of real sources.

Entity-Driven Generation (E2T)

The E2T pipeline generates new implicit texts from entity lists using a large language model (Gemini 2.0 Flash). For each entity, the LLM produces three variant implicit narratives of different styles (factual, emotional/sensory, and historical/cultural). Veterans' entities yield longer first-person recollections; tweet entities yield concise posts. All generated samples are validated for: (a) entity-text relatedness, (b) absence of name leakage (entity name or significant substrings absent from output), and (c) type fidelity. Samples failing any criterion are discarded. This process produced 1,447 veterans and 769 Twitter implicit reference samples before cleaning. E2T data complements T2E by testing whether models can recover entities from systematic, controlled descriptions rather than naturally occurring prose.

Quality Control and Data Cleaning

Both T2E and E2T data underwent a multi-stage cleaning pipeline:

(1) Name-leak verification. Every sample was checked to ensure that the entity's canonical name and all known aliases are absent from the text. The final benchmark has zero entity name leakage.
(2) Entity-text relatedness validation. An LLM judge assessed whether each text plausibly describes the gold entity. Samples with low relatedness were discarded.
(3) Generic entity removal. Entities consisting of common nouns (e.g., "war," "military"), pronouns (e.g., "I," "they"), or ambiguous generic terms were removed. This step eliminated 27 non-entity labels across both domains.
(4) Abbreviation expansion. Abbreviated entity names were expanded to their canonical forms for consistent matching.
(5) Multi-gold deduplication. Texts that were associated with multiple gold entities were consolidated so that each text maps to a single primary entity, with alternatives recorded separately.

Gold Label Audit

A central innovation in IRC-Bench construction is the comprehensive gold-label audit. We identified 667 samples where all four LLM models (Gemini Flash, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-3.5-turbo, Llama 3.1 8B) unanimously failed to recover the gold entity. Rather than treating these as model failures, we audited them systematically using an LLM judge that assessed whether the gold label was in fact the best answer for the given text. Of 363 samples audited, 323 (89%) were found to contain incorrect or suboptimal gold labels. The most common error categories were: entity labels that were overly specific or overly generic, entity labels that named a different but related entity, and texts where the implicit reference was genuinely ambiguous. This audit resulted in:

(a) 152 samples removed entirely (irrecoverably ambiguous or non-entity labels);
(b) 189 gold labels corrected to the more appropriate entity;
(c) 194 multi-reference alternatives added, where multiple valid entity interpretations exist.

The audit finding that 89% of universal model failures were annotation errors rather than model limitations has significant implications: it suggests that reported model accuracy on other implicit entity benchmarks may similarly underestimate true model capability due to gold-label noise.

Entity Descriptions and Wikidata Linking

To support embedding-based retrieval and knowledge-grounded evaluation, LLM-generated descriptions were produced for all 704 unique entities (431 veterans, 273 Twitter) using Gemini 2.0 Flash. Each description provides a one-paragraph summary of the entity's notable attributes, suitable for semantic comparison with implicit reference texts. Additionally, 785 entities were linked to Wikidata entries, providing canonical QIDs, structured descriptions, and alias sets. This enables QID-based canonical matching in evaluation, where a prediction is correct if it resolves to the same Wikidata entity as the gold label regardless of surface-form variation.

Explicit Entity Baseline

In addition to the four implicit variants, IRC-Bench includes a separate explicit-entity baseline of 1,626 Twitter tweets where entities are mentioned by name (with character-span annotations from the original Hosseini dataset). This baseline is marked with eval_mode=EXPLICIT_BASELINE_ONLY to prevent accidental inclusion in implicit evaluation. It enables measuring the performance gap between explicit and implicit entity recognition using the same Twitter domain and entity types.

Methods

We evaluate five recognition paradigms within a unified framework. All methods receive a text segment \(t_i\) and entity type \(c_i\), and produce a ranked list of \(K\) candidate entities. The paradigms span the spectrum from generative LLM reasoning to discriminative embedding retrieval, with hybrid and fine-tuned approaches occupying intermediate positions.

Figure 2. System architecture. Input text and entity type are processed by five recognition paradigms spanning generative LLM reasoning, constrained generation, embedding retrieval, hybrid RAG, and fine-tuned models. Each produces a ranked candidate list evaluated via five-tier matching and multi-level metrics.

Open-Set LLM Recognition

The open-set paradigm uses direct LLM inference to generate candidate entities without access to a predefined entity list. We evaluate three prompt strategies of increasing sophistication:

Zero-shot (v3). A structured prompt provides: (a) a strict output format requiring exactly three entity name guesses per line; (b) formal definitions of all eight entity types; (c) a Wikipedia-notability criterion instructing the model to prefer entities that would have their own Wikipedia article. Entity type conditioning tailors the prompt (e.g., "What Place is implicitly described?"). No contextualization step is used, following ablation evidence that removing the background generation step improves both accuracy (+1% Hit@1) and efficiency (37% faster).

Few-shot. Five worked examples covering all eight entity types are prepended to the zero-shot prompt. Examples are drawn from the training set and show the expected input/output format, including cases where the implicit reference is highly indirect.

Chain-of-thought. The model is prompted to reason step by step: "Identify the contextual cues in the text. What entity type is expected? Which entity best matches all cues?"

Models evaluated: Gemini 2.0 Flash (google/gemini-2.0-flash-001), Llama 3.1 8B Instruct (meta-llama/llama-3.1-8b-instruct), GPT-4o-mini (gpt-4o-mini), and GPT-3.5-turbo (gpt-3.5-turbo). All models use temperature 0.0 and return \(K = 3\) ranked candidates.

Closed-Set Constrained Recognition

The closed-set paradigm provides the model with the complete entity list for the relevant domain, converting the open-ended generation task into a constrained selection problem. Because the full entity list (up to 505 entities) exceeds single-prompt context limits for some models, we employ a chunked two-pass approach:

Pass 1 (chunked retrieval). The entity list is split into chunks of 50 entities. For each chunk, the LLM is asked: "Which of these entities, if any, is described by the text? Return the single best match or NONE." This produces a shortlist of up to one candidate per chunk.

Pass 2 (re-ranking). Candidates from Pass 1 are consolidated and re-ranked by the LLM in a single prompt, producing the final top-3 ranking.

Closed-set recognition dramatically reduces hallucination by constraining the output space to known entities. The primary trade-off is increased API cost (one call per chunk plus the re-ranking call) and the assumption that the gold entity is present in the provided list.

Embedding-Based Retrieval

Text segments are encoded into dense vector representations and compared against a pre-indexed entity embedding space. The top \(K = 10\) nearest neighbors by cosine similarity form the candidate list. We evaluate four embedding models spanning different architectures and training objectives:

MiniLM-L6-v2 (all-MiniLM-L6-v2): a compact 22M-parameter sentence transformer optimized for semantic similarity [sentence-transformers].
MPNet-base-v2 (all-mpnet-base-v2): a 110M-parameter model with improved sentence-level representations.
BGE-base-en-v1.5 (BAAI/bge-base-en-v1.5): a 110M-parameter model trained specifically for retrieval tasks with contrastive learning.
E5-base-v2 (intfloat/e5-base-v2): a 110M-parameter text embedding model trained with weakly supervised contrastive pre-training.

Three entity representation strategies are compared: (a) bare name, embedding only the entity string; (b) description, embedding the LLM-generated entity description (available for all 704 entities); and (c) type-prefixed, prepending the entity type to the name or description. The description representation is expected to provide richer semantic content for matching against implicit narrative text.

As a diagnostic baseline, we also evaluate a mention-span extraction approach inspired by BLINK [72]: the LLM first extracts the most informative span from the text (the "implicit mention"), and this span alone is embedded for retrieval. This tests whether focusing on salient phrases improves retrieval over full-text embedding.

Hybrid RAG Recognition

The hybrid approach combines embedding retrieval with LLM reasoning in a two-stage pipeline. First, the top \(K = 20\) candidate entities are retrieved via embedding similarity (using entity descriptions). Second, these candidates are presented to the LLM alongside the original text for re-ranking with reasoning. The LLM selects and ranks the top 3 entities from the candidate pool, leveraging its ability to reason over distributed cues while benefiting from the retrieval stage's broad coverage. This pipeline tests whether grounding LLM reasoning in retrieved candidates improves over both pure retrieval and pure generation.

Fine-Tuned Methods

Three fine-tuning approaches adapt pre-trained models to the IRC task using the training split:

DPR bi-encoder. Following the Dense Passage Retrieval framework [70], we fine-tune sentence transformer models (MiniLM-L6-v2, BGE-base-en-v1.5, E5-base-v2) using Multiple Negatives Ranking Loss (MNRL). The query encoder processes implicit text segments; the passage encoder processes entity descriptions. Training uses in-batch negatives with a batch size of 32 for 5 epochs. This learns a shared embedding space where implicit texts are pulled closer to their gold entity descriptions.

Cross-encoder re-ranker. A cross-encoder model receives concatenated (text, entity description) pairs and produces a relevance score. Unlike bi-encoders, cross-encoders can attend jointly to both inputs, capturing fine-grained interactions. The cross-encoder re-ranks candidates retrieved by the bi-encoder.

LoRA LLM fine-tuning. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is applied to Llama 3.2 1B to fine-tune the model specifically for implicit entity recognition. The model is trained to generate the correct entity name given the implicit text and entity type, using the training split as supervision. This tests whether task-specific fine-tuning of a smaller LLM can match or exceed the performance of larger zero-shot models.

Evaluation Framework

We introduce a multi-level evaluation framework that addresses the inherent ambiguity of implicit entity references. Evaluation proceeds through three stages: matching, metric computation, and semantic assessment.

Five-Tier Matching Hierarchy

A prediction \(\hat{e}_{i,k}\) is matched against the gold entity \(e_i\) through five tiers applied in order:

(1) Exact match: normalized string equality after lowercasing, Unicode normalization, article/punctuation removal, and whitespace collapsing.
(2) Containment match: one string is a substring of the other after normalization (e.g., "Statue of Liberty" matches "The Statue of Liberty").
(3) Jaccard match: token-level Jaccard similarity exceeds threshold \(\tau = 0.60\):

$$J(\hat{e}_{i,k},\; e_i) = \frac{|\text{tokens}(\hat{e}_{i,k}) \cap \text{tokens}(e_i)|}{|\text{tokens}(\hat{e}_{i,k}) \cup \text{tokens}(e_i)|} \geq \tau$$

(4) WordNet synonym match: the prediction and gold entity share a common synset in WordNet, capturing lexical paraphrases (e.g., "WWII" and "Second World War").
(5) LLM alias match: an LLM judge determines whether the prediction and gold entity refer to the same real-world entity (e.g., "D-Day" and "Normandy landings"). This is the most permissive tier and captures culturally grounded aliases.

Multi-reference evaluation is supported: if a sample has alternative gold entities (recorded during the audit), a match against any alternative also counts as correct.

Ranking Metrics

Performance is assessed at cutoffs \(K \in \{1, 3, 5, 10\}\):

Hit@K measures the proportion of queries where the correct entity appears within the top \(K\) predictions:

$$\text{Hit@K} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}\left[r_i \leq K\right]$$

Precision@K and Recall@K measure the fraction of top-K predictions that are correct and the fraction of relevant entities retrieved, respectively.

nDCG@K (normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) accounts for graded relevance, weighting correct predictions higher when they appear earlier in the ranking.

Mean Reciprocal Rank captures ranking precision. Global MRR averages over all queries (assigning reciprocal rank 0 for misses):

$$\text{MRR}_{\text{global}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\mathbb{1}[r_i < \infty]}{r_i}$$

Filtered MRR averages only over queries with at least one correct retrieval, isolating ranking quality from retrieval coverage:

$$\text{MRR}_{\text{filtered}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{H}|} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{H}} \frac{1}{r_i}, \quad \mathcal{H} = \{i : r_i < \infty\}$$

Semantic Similarity Metrics

Beyond binary match/no-match, we compute graded semantic similarity between predictions and gold entities:

Embedding cosine similarity: cosine distance between the prediction's and gold entity's embedding vectors, capturing distributional semantic proximity.
WordNet Wu-Palmer similarity: path-based similarity in the WordNet taxonomy, measuring conceptual relatedness.
Combined score: the arithmetic mean of embedding cosine and Wu-Palmer similarities, providing a balanced semantic assessment.
Graded relevance: a continuous score in [0, 1] based on the combined semantic similarity, used as the gain function in nDCG computation.

Wikidata Entity Linking Evaluation

For knowledge-grounded assessment, predictions are linked to Wikidata entities (785 benchmark entities have QIDs). A prediction is considered a canonical match if it resolves to the same Wikidata QID as the gold entity, regardless of surface-form variation. This eliminates matching artifacts caused by name variations, abbreviations, or alternative labels.

Three Reporting Levels

Results are reported at three levels of strictness: strict (exact string match only), normalized (all five matching tiers), and graded (semantic similarity with Wikidata canonical matching). This decomposition reveals that 10-15% of apparent failures under strict matching are resolved by the normalized and graded levels, quantifying the contribution of each evaluation component.

Implementation Details and Reproducibility

Model specifications. All LLM experiments specify exact model identifiers: Gemini 2.0 Flash (google/gemini-2.0-flash-001), Llama 3.1 8B Instruct (meta-llama/llama-3.1-8b-instruct via HuggingFace Inference API), GPT-4o-mini and GPT-3.5-turbo (via OpenAI Batch API). All LLM calls use temperature 0.0 for deterministic output. Embedding models use default configurations from the sentence-transformers library. All experiments use a fixed random seed (42) for reproducibility.

Computational cost. LLM-based inference costs approximately $0.02-0.15 per full benchmark evaluation depending on model and prompt strategy. Embedding-based retrieval is orders of magnitude cheaper (under $0.01 for the full benchmark). Closed-set recognition is the most expensive paradigm due to chunked multi-pass inference, costing approximately $0.20-0.50 per benchmark run. Fine-tuning experiments used a single consumer GPU (NVIDIA RTX-class) with training times under 30 minutes per model.

H.4 Entity Linking Coverage

Of the 814 unique entities in the curated benchmark, 785 (96%) were successfully linked to Wikidata entries, providing canonical QIDs, structured descriptions, and alias sets. The remaining 29 unlinked entities are primarily local or obscure references (e.g., specific military units, local landmarks) that lack Wikidata entries. Entity linking enables QID-based canonical matching in evaluation, where predictions that resolve to the same Wikidata entity as the gold label are counted as correct regardless of surface-form differences.

H.5 Text Length Distribution

Text length varies substantially across variants (Figure A4). Veterans T2E narratives are the longest (mean approximately 107 words), reflecting the multi-sentence reminiscence style. Veterans E2T texts are shorter (mean approximately 49 words) as the LLM generates more concise narratives. Twitter T2E tweets average approximately 27 words, and Twitter E2T averages approximately 19 words. This 5.6x range in text length tests whether recognition methods are robust to input length variation.

Within each domain, T2E and E2T variants share most entities, since E2T is generated from the same entity lists. Across domains, overlap is minimal (fewer than 10 shared entities between veterans and Twitter), confirming that the two domains test genuinely different entity knowledge.

H.7 Real vs. Synthetic Data

The curated benchmark contains 1,344 real samples (40.2%) and 2,003 synthetic samples (59.8%). By domain, veterans contribute 1,797 samples (53.7%) and Twitter 1,550 (46.3%). The synthetic-to-real ratio of 1.5:1 reflects the scalability of E2T generation. Key differences between real and synthetic data include: (a) real texts are longer and more narratively complex; (b) synthetic texts use more systematic description patterns; (c) real texts contain cultural and emotional nuances absent from generated text. These differences make the T2E/E2T comparison a natural ablation for studying the effect of text naturalness on recognition difficulty.
